# Founding and Rise of Bethlehem Steel (1857–1916)

## Summary

Bethlehem Steel's trajectory from a small iron works in eastern Pennsylvania to America's second-largest steelmaker is a story of strategic pivots, visionary leadership, and wartime opportunity. Founded in 1857 as the Saucona Iron Company to produce railroad rails, it nearly died in its cradle during the Panic of 1857. Reorganized as the Bethlehem Iron Company in 1861, it built its first blast furnace during the Civil War and spent its first two decades as a regional rail producer. The critical pivot came in the mid-1880s, when declining rail margins and the U.S. Navy's decision to rebuild with steel-hulled warships led the company into heavy forgings and armor plate — a move that transformed it from a commodity producer into a defense-industrial firm.

The second transformation came with Charles M. Schwab. After resigning as president of U.S. Steel in 1903, Schwab acquired Bethlehem Steel out of the wreckage of the United States Shipbuilding Company bankruptcy. He brought Carnegie-style ambition to a firm whose ingot capacity in 1905 represented less than one percent of the national total. His boldest bet — investing in Henry Grey's wide-flange beam rolling process — gave Bethlehem a monopoly on structural steel shapes that powered the skyscraper boom. When World War I erupted, the company was positioned as the Allies' primary munitions supplier in the Western Hemisphere. Revenue surged from $47.7 million in 1914 to $217.9 million in 1916. By war's end, Bethlehem had earned $110 million in war profits alone, six times more than the preceding nine years combined, and Schwab had built the clear number-two steel company in America.

## Key Findings

### Phase 1: The Bethlehem Iron Company (1857–1899)

**Founding and early struggles.** In 1857, Augustus Wolle, a Moravian minister turned industrialist, pitched the idea of building an iron company in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania. The resulting Saucona Iron Company was immediately hit by the Panic of 1857, halting further organization. The company reorganized, moved to South Bethlehem (strategically positioned at the junction of the North Pennsylvania and Lehigh Valley Railroads), and renamed itself the Bethlehem Rolling Mill and Iron Company. On June 14, 1860, Alfred Hunt was elected president. On May 1, 1861, it became the Bethlehem Iron Company.[^1][^2]

**John Fritz and the rail business.** In 1860, Robert Sayre, chief engineer of the Lehigh Valley Railroad, recruited John Fritz — "the most inventive of all American iron masters" — to design and run the works. Fritz arrived July 5, 1860, on a salary of $5,000/year plus 40 shares of company stock. He brought his patent for the three-high rail mill (patented 1858). Construction of the first blast furnace began July 1, 1861; it became operational January 4, 1863. The first railroad rails were produced September 26, 1863.[^2][^3]

**The pivot to ordnance and armor.** By the 1880s, competition from Pittsburgh and Scranton firms was squeezing the rail business. The inflection point came when Congress, in spring 1886, authorized construction of steel-hulled warships including the battleships USS Texas and USS Maine. In 1885, John Fritz and directors Robert H. Sayre and Joseph Wharton met with Lt. William Jaques to explore ordnance production, establishing a partnership with Joseph Whitworth's firm in Manchester, England. On June 28, 1887, Bethlehem secured contracts for both gun forgings and armor plate for the Texas and Maine.[^1][^4]

**Building the forging plant.** Between 1888 and 1892, the company completed the first U.S. heavy-forging plant, designed by Fritz with assistance from Russell Davenport (who joined in 1888). By fall 1890, Bethlehem Iron was delivering gun forgings to the Navy. A contract from March 1892 shows the company supplying 59 breech-loading rifled guns (4" and 5" diameters) over seven months.[^1][^5]

**The Bessemer Process.** In 1873, Bethlehem Iron became the first company in the region to use the Bessemer Process, converting molten pig iron into steel — reportedly 16 times more durable than iron.[^6]

**Armor plate controversy.** In 1893–94, both Bethlehem Iron and Carnegie Steel were implicated in an armor plate scandal. The issue was concealing defects ("blowholes") from government inspectors. Charles Schwab, then superintendent of Carnegie's Homestead Works, testified that blowholes were inevitable in first-class armor and argued the Navy faced a choice between strong armor with blowholes or weak armor without.[^7]

**Reorganization.** In 1899, Joseph Wharton (majority stockholder and founder of the Wharton School at the University of Pennsylvania) and Robert Packer Linderman reorganized the business as the Bethlehem Steel Company to assume liabilities of the Bethlehem Iron Company.[^1]

### Phase 2: The Schwab Era (1901–1916)

**Schwab's background.** Charles Michael Schwab (born February 18, 1862, Williamsburg, PA) began his career as a stake-driver at Carnegie's Edgar Thomson Steel Works in Braddock, PA. He rose to superintendent of Homestead Works (1887), then Edgar Thomson (1890), and at age 35 became president of Carnegie Steel Company (1897). In December 1900, he brokered the sale of Carnegie Steel to J.P. Morgan, acting as intermediary. When the United States Steel Corporation was formed in 1901, Schwab became its first president at age 39.[^8][^9]

**The U.S. Shipbuilding debacle.** Schwab acquired a controlling interest in Bethlehem Steel in 1901. Shortly after, a group of investors proposed merging several shipbuilders into a trust — the United States Shipbuilding Company. Schwab offered to sell Bethlehem for $9 million cash; the promoters, having no cash, offered $30 million in stocks and bonds instead. Schwab accepted. Most of the acquired shipyards were badly run and losing money. Within a year, U.S. Shipbuilding was bankrupt. After lawsuits and negative press, Schwab ended up back in control of the company, now again called Bethlehem Steel.[^10]

**Departure from U.S. Steel.** Schwab's conflicts with J.P. Morgan and fellow executive Elbert Gary led to his resignation from U.S. Steel in 1903. He then devoted himself fully to building Bethlehem Steel.[^8]

**Incorporation.** In December 1904, Bethlehem Steel Corporation was formally incorporated, with Schwab as president and chairman. Initial holdings included the steel company, a Cuban iron ore mine, and shipbuilding facilities in California and Delaware. Starting ingot capacity represented less than one percent of the national total.[^11]

**The Grey Mill and the Bethlehem Beam.** Schwab's defining strategic bet. Henry Grey had patented a rolling process (1897) using horizontal and vertical rollers to produce wide-flange structural shapes (H-beams, I-beams) from a single steel ingot. Grey first brought his design to U.S. Steel, but its finance committee rejected it. Schwab invested heavily in the technology. When challenged on the risk, he reportedly declared: "If we are going bust, we will go bust big." In January 1908, the Grey Mill began commercial production. The beams were lighter, stronger, and cheaper than traditional riveted built-up sections. The first major project was Gimbel's department store in New York City, using 12,000 tons of Grey beams. Bethlehem held a monopoly on wide-flange beams until 1927, when U.S. Steel began rolling "Carnegie Beams" under license. The Grey Mill ran for nearly 90 years until steel production in Bethlehem ceased in 1995.[^12][^13][^14]

**Eugene Grace.** An electrical engineer hired early in Schwab's tenure, Grace proved such an effective manager that Schwab could focus on strategy and external affairs. Grace was named president in 1916, with Schwab remaining as chairman. Grace would lead the company for the next 29 years (until 1945).[^11][^15]

**Pre-war diversification.** Under Schwab, Bethlehem acquired an interest in a Chilean iron ore mine, established an ocean transportation business with its own ore carrier fleet, and in 1913 acquired the Fore River Shipbuilding Company in Quincy, Massachusetts.[^1][^11]

### Phase 3: World War I Catalyst (1914–1916)

**Revenue explosion.** Bethlehem Steel's revenues surged from $47.7 million (1914) to $147.6 million (1915) to $217.9 million (1916). During the war years overall, the company earned $110 million — six times more than the preceding nine years combined.[^1][^16]

**Stock price.** Shares soared from approximately $30 before the war to $600 during it. On October 4, 1915, the stock hit $399.[^16][^17]

**Allied contracts.** The company took a $135 million order from Britain — the largest in its history to that point. Bethlehem built warships for Great Britain at company shipyards and filled orders for guns, munitions, armor, and ordnance from Britain, France, and Russia.[^1][^11]

**War production totals** (from a postwar U.S. government report): 11,000 gun barrels, 18 million artillery shells, 34,000 tons of armor plate.[^16]

**Workforce.** South Bethlehem was packed with 31,000 workers producing guns, shells, armor plate, submarine parts, and other materiel.[^16]

**1916 acquisitions.** Bolstered by wartime profits, Bethlehem acquired the American Iron and Steel Manufacturing Company, the Pennsylvania Steel Company, and the Maryland Steel Company, significantly expanding its capacity and geographic reach.[^1][^11]

**Tripled output.** Under Grace's operational leadership, Bethlehem tripled its steel output over the course of the war.[^15]

## Conflicting Views / Debates

**1. Schwab's acquisition timeline.** Sources differ on whether Schwab acquired Bethlehem Steel in 1901 or 1903. The most consistent account is that he acquired a controlling interest in 1901 (while still at U.S. Steel), lost and then regained control through the U.S. Shipbuilding bankruptcy in 1902–03, and formally incorporated Bethlehem Steel Corporation in December 1904 after resigning from U.S. Steel in 1903. Some sources conflate the formal incorporation date with the acquisition date.

**2. "First" Bessemer Process claim.** The Pennsylvania Center for the Book claims Bethlehem was "the first to use the Bessemer Process" in 1873, but this conflicts with the well-documented fact that Alexander Holley's Troy, NY works and the Pennsylvania Steel Company in Steelton, PA were using the Bessemer process years earlier (mid-1860s). Bethlehem may have been the first *in the Lehigh Valley region*, not nationally.

**3. Henry Grey's patent and the mill's start date.** Most sources cite January 1908 for commercial production, but the PA Center for the Book says 1907 was when Bethlehem "first adopted" Grey's process — possibly referring to the beginning of construction vs. first commercial rolling.

**4. The armor plate scandal.** Whether the blowholes constituted genuine fraud or were an industry-standard practice remains debated. Schwab's defense — that eliminating blowholes would weaken the plate — was technically defensible but did not address the concealment from inspectors. Both Carnegie and Bethlehem were implicated, suggesting an industry-wide practice.

**5. Schwab's motivations for leaving U.S. Steel.** Some accounts emphasize personal conflicts with Morgan and Gary; others suggest Schwab was already planning to build his own independent steel empire and chafed at the bureaucratic conservatism of U.S. Steel's management.

## Open Questions

1. **What was Bethlehem Steel's actual ingot capacity (in tons) in 1905 vs. 1916?** Sources confirm it was "less than one percent" of national capacity in 1905 but do not give an absolute tonnage figure. U.S. national ingot production was approximately 20 million tons in 1905, implying Bethlehem produced under 200,000 tons — but this needs verification.

2. **What were the terms of the 1887 Navy contract?** The dollar value of the original USS Texas/USS Maine forging and armor contracts has not been located in accessible sources.

3. **How many employees did Bethlehem have before WWI?** The 31,000 figure is clearly wartime. Pre-war workforce numbers would help quantify the war's impact on employment growth.

4. **What was the exact structure of Schwab's 1901 acquisition?** The relationship between his personal purchase, the U.S. Shipbuilding Company involvement, and the eventual 1904 incorporation involves financial complexity that public sources describe inconsistently.

5. **What was the nature of the Whitworth partnership?** The 1885 arrangement with Joseph Whitworth's Manchester firm for ordnance technology is mentioned but not detailed — was it a licensing deal, joint venture, or equipment purchase?

6. **How did Bethlehem's labor relations compare to Carnegie/U.S. Steel in this period?** Schwab maintained anti-union policies; Bethlehem was not unionized until 1941. But what were working conditions and wages like during the 1900–1916 expansion?

7. **What was the profitability of the Grey beam monopoly?** Bethlehem was the sole producer of wide-flange beams from 1908 to 1927 — what were margins on structural steel vs. other product lines?

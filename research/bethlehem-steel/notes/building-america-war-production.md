# Building America & War Production

## Summary

Bethlehem Steel was not merely a steel producer -- it was the company that physically built modern America and armed it for two world wars. From 1908 onward, its invention of the wide-flange structural beam enabled the skyscraper age, and its steel went into the most iconic structures of the 20th century: the Golden Gate Bridge, the Empire State Building, the George Washington Bridge, Rockefeller Center, and dozens more. In wartime, the company transformed into the nation's most important defense contractor. During World War I, it held a near-monopoly on Allied munitions supply, producing over 1.1 billion pounds of shell steel and 20.1 million rounds of artillery ammunition. During World War II, its 15 shipyards built 1,121 ships -- nearly one-fifth of the U.S. Navy's two-ocean fleet -- while producing 73.4 million tons of steel, roughly one-third of all U.S. armor plate and gun forgings. At peak wartime production in 1943, the company employed 300,000 workers and launched 380 ships in a single year, fulfilling Eugene Grace's audacious promise to FDR of "one ship per day." By 1945, Bethlehem was the nation's largest defense contractor and produced more steel than the entire output of England. The postwar transition, however, planted the seeds of decline: flush with cash and complacent from guaranteed demand, Bethlehem failed to invest in new steelmaking technologies (basic oxygen furnaces, continuous casting) that Japan and Germany were adopting, setting the stage for its eventual unraveling.

## Key Findings

### The Wide-Flange Beam Revolution

Bethlehem's most consequential structural innovation was the wide-flange beam, first rolled in January 1908 using Henry Grey's patented rolling mill process (patent 1897). These beams -- shaped from a single piece of steel through a series of rolls -- replaced earlier riveted, built-up construction methods and allowed buildings to climb above 20 stories economically. The first major project using Grey beams was Gimbel's department store in New York City (12,000 tons of Grey beams). This innovation made Bethlehem the dominant supplier of structural steel to the construction industry and, as one company engineer put it, meant that "Bethlehem Steel owned New York" -- the company supplied steel for roughly 80% of the New York skyline.[^1]

### Iconic Structures Built with Bethlehem Steel

**Bridges:**
- Golden Gate Bridge, San Francisco (completed 1937; steel fabricated through McClintic-Marshall subsidiary)
- George Washington Bridge, New York (completed 1931)
- Verrazzano-Narrows Bridge, New York (Staten Island tower)
- Ben Franklin Bridge, Philadelphia (completed 1926)
- Commodore Barry Bridge, Pennsylvania-New Jersey
- Peace Bridge, Buffalo-Fort Erie, Ontario

**Skyscrapers and Buildings:**
- Empire State Building, New York (completed 1931)
- Chrysler Building, New York (completed 1930)
- Rockefeller Center, New York (1930s)
- Waldorf-Astoria Hotel, New York (completed 1931)
- Madison Square Garden, New York
- 28 Liberty Street (formerly Chase Manhattan Plaza), New York (53,000-ton steel frame)
- One Chase Manhattan Plaza, New York
- Merchandise Mart, Chicago
- John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts, Washington, D.C.
- U.S. Supreme Court Building, Washington, D.C.

**Dams and Infrastructure:**
- Hoover Dam (completed 1936)
- Grand Coulee Dam (completed 1942)
- Bonneville Dam (completed 1938)
- Lincoln Tunnel, New York (3rd tube, 1950s -- over 28,000 cast segments)
- Alcatraz Island federal penitentiary

**Other Notable Projects:**
- World's first Ferris Wheel axle (1893 Chicago World's Fair) -- 45.5-foot steel axle, largest single steel forging ever constructed at the time
- Wonder Wheel, Coney Island
- Largest electric generator shaft (1950s, for General Electric)

**Notable Loss:** In 1967, Bethlehem lost its bid to supply steel for the World Trade Center, with the 50,000-ton contract going to competitors. This was considered a significant blow and early sign of competitive decline.[^2]

### The McClintic-Marshall Acquisition (1931)

Bethlehem's dominance in structural steel fabrication was cemented by its $32 million acquisition of McClintic-Marshall Corporation in 1931. McClintic-Marshall was a major structural steel fabricator specializing in bridges and large buildings. This acquisition enabled Bethlehem to participate directly in constructing the Golden Gate Bridge, Rockefeller Plaza, the Waldorf-Astoria, the Merchandise Mart, and the U.S. Supreme Court Building.[^3]

### World War I Production (1914-1918)

When war broke out in Europe in 1914, Bethlehem held a virtual monopoly on certain categories of Allied munitions. Under Charles M. Schwab's leadership, the company filled approximately $500 million in Allied orders throughout the conflict.

**Pre-war baseline (1914):**
- Annual steelmaking capacity: 1.1 million tons
- Employees: 15,600

**Wartime production:**
- 65,000 pounds of forged military products
- 70 million pounds of armor plate
- 1.1 billion pounds of steel for shells
- 20.1 million rounds of artillery ammunition for Britain and France
- Warships built for Great Britain at company shipyards
- Guns, munitions, armor, and ordnance for British, French, and Russian forces

**Workforce:** 31,000 workers at the South Bethlehem plant alone, producing guns, shells, armor plate, submarine parts, and other materiel.

**Financial impact:** Bethlehem averaged 24% return on investment after taxes during the war years. The $135 million British order was the largest single contract in company history to that point. In 1916, bolstered by wartime profits, Bethlehem acquired the American Iron and Steel Manufacturing Company, Pennsylvania Steel Company, and Maryland Steel Company -- transforming from a niche producer into a major integrated steelmaker.[^4][^5]

**Controversy:** Schwab and Bethlehem were later branded "merchants of death" for their wartime profiteering. Schwab circumvented American neutrality laws by funneling goods through Canada. Government charges were eventually repudiated by the courts.[^6]

### World War II Production (1940-1945)

WWII transformed Bethlehem into the nation's largest defense contractor, ranked seventh among all U.S. corporations in wartime production contract value.

**Steel production:**
- Total output 1940-1945: 73.4 million tons
- This exceeded the entire steel output of England
- Approximately one-third of all U.S. armor plate and gun forgings

**Specific military production percentages:**
- 70% of airplane cylinder forgings for U.S. armed forces
- 25% of armor plate for warships
- 33% of large cannon forgings for U.S. armed forces

**Other military products:**
- Bomb casings and armor-piercing shells
- Airplane parts and engines
- Airfield landing mats
- Submarine air flasks
- Railway equipment
- Tank forgings and cable
- Nearly $1.5 billion in contracts for bomb casings, gun forgings, airplane parts, and other military supplies

**Financial scale:**
- Pre-war gross sales: $135 million
- 1945 sales: $1.33 billion (roughly 10x increase)

**Employment:**
- 1943 peak: 283,765 employees (approximately 300,000 at absolute peak)
- Approximately 25,000 were women
- 67,000 Bethlehem employees served in the armed forces
- 180,000 employees in shipbuilding operations alone[^7][^8]

### Naval Shipbuilding

The Bethlehem Shipbuilding Corporation, created in 1905 with the acquisition of Union Iron Works and formally incorporated in 1917, became the largest of the "Big Three" U.S. shipbuilders by 1940 -- the only one capable of constructing any vessel type.

**WWII overall:**
- 15 shipyards across the country
- 1,121 ships built (more than any other builder in WWII)
- Nearly one-fifth of the U.S. Navy's two-ocean fleet
- 37,778 vessels repaired, converted, or serviced
- In 1943 alone: 380 ships launched, exceeding Grace's "one ship per day" promise to FDR by 15 ships

**Major Shipyard Production:**

| Shipyard | Location | Key Production | Ships Built |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bethlehem-Fairfield | Baltimore, MD | Liberty ships, LSTs, Victory ships | 523 total (384 Liberty ships, 45 LSTs, 94 Victory ships) |
| Fore River Shipyard | Quincy, MA | Aircraft carriers, battleships, cruisers, DEs, LSTs | 220+ (surpassed all yards in tonnage delivered) |
| Bethlehem-Hingham | Hingham, MA | Destroyer escorts, LSTs | ~277 (132 DEs, 165 LSTs) |
| San Pedro Shipyard | Los Angeles, CA | Destroyers | 26 destroyers (Benson, Fletcher, Allen M. Sumner, Gearing classes) |
| Staten Island (Mariners Harbor) | New York, NY | Various naval vessels | Active 1938-1963 |

**Notable Capital Ships Built at Fore River:**
- USS Lexington (CV-2) -- battlecruiser-turned-aircraft carrier
- USS Massachusetts (BB-59) -- battleship
- USS Bunker Hill (CV-17) -- Essex-class carrier
- USS Wasp (CV-18) -- Essex-class carrier
- USS Hancock (CV-19) -- Essex-class carrier
- Seven Baltimore-class heavy cruisers
- USS Salem (CA-139) -- Des Moines-class cruiser
- 18 destroyer escorts
- 46 LSTs

**The Fairfield Miracle:** The Bethlehem-Fairfield shipyard in Baltimore was purpose-built in 1941 for emergency wartime production. Its 13 slipways (later 16) were serviced by 75 cranes, 10 locomotives, and 200+ flatcars, using modular construction where components were fabricated in shops and transported to slipways for assembly. It launched the SS Patrick Henry -- the first Liberty ship -- on September 27, 1941, with President Roosevelt present. The yard employed 27,000 workers at peak.

**Hingham Speed Record:** The Bethlehem-Hingham shipyard delivered a destroyer escort in just 23 days, employed 30,000 workers, and built 90 escort vessels in the single year of 1943.[^9][^10][^11]

### Eugene Grace's "Ship a Day" Promise

In early 1943, Bethlehem Steel president Eugene Grace (who led the company 1913-1945 as president, then 1946-1957 as chairman) publicly declared his goal of building one ship per day across all Bethlehem yards. Skeptics called it impossible. Grace delivered: 380 ships in 1943, or roughly 1.04 ships per day. On December 31, 1943, Grace addressed workers via radio from the Staten Island shipyard, citing 67,000 Bethlehem employees serving in the armed forces and calling the production achievement "the miracle of American production that spelled the doom of the Axis powers."[^12]

### Postwar Transition and Challenges

The end of WWII presented Bethlehem with a paradoxical situation: unprecedented financial strength but structural vulnerabilities that would prove fatal.

**Immediate postwar adjustments:**
- Female workers were "promptly fired in favor of male counterparts"
- Shipyards transitioned to commercial work; some were converted to other industries
- Demand remained strong as rebuilding nations needed steel

**The 1950s peak and complacency:**
- Averaged 23 million tons of steel per year through the 1950s
- 1957: Produced 19 million tons; earned $190 million on $2.6 billion in sales
- 165,000 full-time employees
- Secured contracts for nuclear and aerospace industries
- Steel was in such demand that "American mills could sell all they produced at almost any price"

**Three critical failures (identified by historians):**
1. **Labor relations:** Dismal management-worker relations created recurring conflicts
2. **Executive excess over innovation:** Instead of investing in new technology, the company spent heavily on executive perks (the infamous Grace bonus system had distributed $36 million in bonuses since 1911)
3. **Technology lag:** Japan and Germany rebuilt war-damaged plants with basic-oxygen furnaces and continuous casting -- technologies that produced higher-quality steel at substantially lower cost. Bethlehem was slow to adopt both.

**The 1967 turning point:** Losing the World Trade Center steel contract to competitors signaled that Bethlehem was no longer the automatic choice. Within a decade, imported steel was considerably cheaper than domestic production.[^13][^14]

## Conflicting Views / Debates

**Ship count discrepancies:** Sources disagree on exact WWII shipbuilding totals. Wikipedia and most sources cite 1,121 ships; the ExplorePAHistory marker for Eugene Grace cites 1,085 vessels. The Hingham shipyard count varies between 227 and 277 depending on source. These discrepancies likely reflect different counting methods (whether to include ships completed after VJ Day, vessels under repair vs. new construction, etc.).

**"Merchants of death" debate:** Bethlehem's WWI profiteering was a major political controversy. The Nye Committee hearings of 1934-36 investigated munitions makers including Bethlehem. Charles Schwab was accused of circumventing neutrality laws by routing arms through Canada. Courts eventually repudiated government charges, but the moral question of profiting from wartime production persisted throughout the 20th century.

**Wartime contract value ranking:** Bethlehem is described as both "ranked seventh among all U.S. corporations in wartime production contract value" and "the nation's largest defense contractor" by war's end. These are not necessarily contradictory -- contract value and total production volume are different measures, and Bethlehem's in-kind steel production may have exceeded the dollar value of its formal contracts.

**Postwar blame:** Historians debate whether Bethlehem's decline was primarily caused by management complacency, labor costs, foreign competition, or failure to innovate. Most agree it was a combination, but the relative weight varies. Kenneth Warren's "Bethlehem Steel: Builder and Arsenal of America" emphasizes strategic management failures, while labor historians point to the adversarial relationship between management and the United Steelworkers.

## Open Questions

1. **Exact total ship count:** The discrepancy between 1,085 and 1,121 ships needs resolution. What is the definitive count, and what accounting differences explain the gap?

2. **WWI ship production specifics:** While WWII shipbuilding data is relatively well-documented, specific numbers for WWI naval construction by Bethlehem are harder to pin down. How many warships did Bethlehem build for Britain and the U.S. during 1914-1918?

3. **Steel tonnage in specific structures:** How much Bethlehem steel went into each iconic structure? The 53,000-ton figure for Chase Manhattan Plaza is rare -- similar figures for the Empire State Building, Golden Gate Bridge, etc. would be valuable.

4. **Wartime profitability:** While 1945 sales of $1.33 billion are documented, what were actual profit margins during WWII? Were they comparable to the 24% ROI of WWI, or did wartime price controls and excess profits taxes reduce them?

5. **Uranium fuel rod production:** Bethlehem rolled uranium fuel rods for nuclear reactors at Lackawanna, NY (1949-1952). What was the scale of this program, and how did it connect to Cold War defense production?

6. **Competitive dynamics with US Steel during wartime:** Both companies were critical to the war effort. How were contracts divided? Did the government actively manage allocation between them?

7. **Technology transfer from wartime:** Did Bethlehem's wartime innovations in modular shipbuilding, mass production, or metallurgy transfer to peacetime operations, or were they largely abandoned?

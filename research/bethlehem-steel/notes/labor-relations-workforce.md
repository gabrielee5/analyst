# Labor Relations & Workforce — Bethlehem Steel

## Summary

Bethlehem Steel's labor history is a microcosm of American industrial labor relations across the 20th century. From the brutal 12-hour days and 84-hour weeks of the early 1900s, through decades of violent strike suppression and company unionism, to the eventual triumph of the United Steelworkers — and then the ironic endgame where generous labor contracts became an anchor dragging the company into bankruptcy. The arc runs from exploitation to organization to middle-class prosperity to competitive collapse, with workers bearing the costs at both ends.

The company resisted independent unionization for over three decades (1910–1941), using state police, tear gas, company unions, espionage, and strikebreakers. When unions finally won recognition in 1941, steelworker wages rose to 60% above average manufacturing wages — a premium that, combined with underfunded pensions and rigid work rules, helped render Bethlehem uncompetitive against foreign producers and domestic mini-mills by the 1980s. The final indignity: 95,000 retirees lost their health and life insurance benefits when the company dissolved in 2003, and the PBGC took over a pension plan that was only 45% funded.

## Key Findings

### The 1910 Strike: A Failed Protest That Changed Federal Law

The strike began on **February 4, 1910**, when three machinists were fired for protesting working conditions. It escalated to approximately **9,000 workers** walking out (though only ~2,200 held out for the duration). The strike lasted **108 days**, ending May 18, 1910, when leaders voted to return with virtually no concessions won.[^1]

**Core grievances:**
- Laborers earned **12.5 cents per hour** (~$4.30 in modern dollars)
- Machine shop workers averaged **57-hour weeks**; blast furnace and boiler workers worked **84-hour weeks** (12 hours/day, 7 days/week)
- Over a quarter of the workforce worked Sundays with no additional compensation
- No overtime pay at any premium rate
- The "bonus system" (designed by Frederick Winslow Taylor) paid skilled workers so little in base wages that they were forced to work excessive overtime

**Key events:**
- **February 26, 1910:** State police charged into a crowd of bystanders, beating men, women, and children. **Josef Szambo**, a Hungarian immigrant steelworker, was **shot and killed** by a state trooper.
- Charles M. Schwab refused to negotiate, arguing the strikers were no longer employees.
- Workers published demands on March 5: wage increases, time-and-a-half for overtime, paid holidays, just cause for terminations, and a standard 10-hour day.

**Lasting impact:** The strike prompted a **Bureau of Labor investigation** whose report (released May 4, 1910) confirmed over half the workforce worked 12-hour shifts. The press coverage "aroused the nation." In **June 1912**, President Taft signed **HR 9061** — the first federal law regulating working hours in private industry, requiring 8-hour days on federal contracts.[^2]

### The 1919 Steel Strike

The national steel strike began **September 22, 1919**, involving **365,000 workers** across the Midwest and East, including Bethlehem. At Bethlehem, approximately **6,000 workers** walked out. Core demands centered on the 12-hour day, the right to organize, and living wages.[^3]

The strike lasted until **January 8, 1920**, and was a **total failure**. The Interchurch World Movement concluded: *"The United States Steel Corporation was too big to be beaten by 300,000 workingmen."* In Pennsylvania, state police **clubbed picketers, dragged strikers from their homes, and jailed thousands on flimsy charges**. There was a complete suppression of civil liberties in the steel towns.[^4]

The strike's defeat cemented open-shop dominance in steel for nearly two decades.

### The Company Union Era (1918–1937)

After the 1919 defeat, Bethlehem Steel created the **Employees' Representation Plan (ERP)** in 1918, designed by **William Mackenzie King** (later Canadian Prime Minister) for Eugene Grace. The ERP was a classic company union: workers could register grievances but without independent union representation.[^5]

The ERP was not entirely toothless — worker representatives negotiated a **work-sharing program** during the Depression to mitigate layoffs, convinced management to create an emergency employment program in the early 1930s, and won a **paid vacation program in 1936**. But the plan's fundamental purpose was to prevent independent organization.[^5]

The Supreme Court's upholding of the **National Labor Relations Act (Wagner Act) in 1937** outlawed company unions, forcing Bethlehem to dismantle its ERP.

### The Little Steel Strike (1937)

SWOC (the Steel Workers Organizing Committee, founded **June 7, 1936**, under Philip Murray and backed by John L. Lewis's CIO) had won a surprise recognition from U.S. Steel in March 1937. But the "Little Steel" companies — Republic Steel, Bethlehem Steel, Youngstown Sheet & Tube, National Steel, Inland Steel, and American Rolling Mills — refused.[^6]

SWOC called a strike on **May 26, 1937**, eventually involving **over 80,000 workers**. The companies used violence, espionage, and strikebreakers. The most notorious incident was the **Memorial Day Massacre (May 30, 1937)** at Republic Steel's South Chicago plant, where police killed **10 strikers** and wounded approximately **100** — many shot in the back while fleeing. While this occurred at Republic Steel, Bethlehem was part of the same coordinated industry resistance.[^7]

The 1937 strike was called off as a failure. But NLRB rulings and court orders accumulated against the Little Steel companies over the following years.

### The 1941 Strike and Union Recognition

The decisive breakthrough came on **March 24, 1941**, when SWOC organized a walkout at Bethlehem Steel. By 11 PM, an estimated **2,500 people** — strikers, women, and children — were gathered at the main gate.[^8]

**Violence erupted** when a Bethlehem city police car **lobbed a tear gas bomb** into the crowd. Enraged workers charged into the company parking lot and **overturned 20 cars**. Governor Arthur James called in **125 additional state police**. Over March 25–26, state police repeatedly charged strikers and were met with **bricks and bottles**; troopers were thrown to the ground, police car windshields were smashed.[^8]

SWOC field director **John Riffe** urged restraint. State and federal mediators intervened, and the **Roosevelt administration** pressured for settlement. An agreement was announced: all strikers returned to work, and the company agreed to collective bargaining with SWOC. By **August 1941**, Bethlehem Steel formally recognized SWOC as the workers' representative. SWOC reorganized as the **United Steelworkers of America (USWA) in 1942**.[^9]

### The 12-Hour Day Debate

The 12-hour day was the steel industry's most contentious labor issue from 1900 to the mid-1920s. At Bethlehem Steel:
- Blast furnace and boiler workers worked **12-hour shifts, 7 days a week = 84 hours/week**
- Plant superintendent **Eugene Grace** argued that *"the eight-hour workday would be entirely out of the question"*
- The 1910 Bureau of Labor report found conditions "shocking"
- The federal 8-hour law of 1912 applied only to government contract work
- Industry-wide adoption of the 8-hour day came gradually through the 1920s, partly under pressure from a **1923 campaign** by President Harding and public opinion[^10]

### Wages, Benefits, and the Competitive Burden

**Executive vs. Worker Pay Disparity:**
In **1929**, CEO Eugene Grace received a bonus exceeding **$1.6 million** (about 3.3% of earnings). A stockholder lawsuit in 1931 challenged **$36 million in bonuses** distributed since 1911 — settled without returning funds but forcing disclosure of executive pay. Unions subsequently used the bonus issue to demand higher worker compensation.[^11]

**Postwar Wage Escalation:**
Multiple steel strikes (1946, 1948, 1952, 1955, 1956, 1959) drove up wages. By the 1970s–80s, steelworker wages were running **60% above the average factory worker** (vs. the historical 30% premium). This made integrated producers like Bethlehem uncompetitive against foreign steel and domestic mini-mills with lower labor costs and more flexible work rules.[^12]

**Pension Time Bomb:**
Eugene Grace **failed to adequately fund pension plans during the 1950s**. As the workforce aged, the ratio of retirees to active workers inverted — defined-benefit pensions required funding regardless of business performance, creating a fixed cost that could not adjust downward. The pension plan was ultimately only **45% funded**: $3.5 billion in assets against **$7.8 billion in liabilities**.[^13]

### Safety and Occupational Hazards

Steel work was extraordinarily dangerous:
- **Over 500 workers died on the job between 1905 and 1941** at Bethlehem Steel
- Hundreds or thousands more were badly injured by burning metal, toxic gases, and fast-moving machinery
- At Sparrows Point alone, **1982–1990: 26 major accidents, 10 workers killed, 37 injured**
- **Asbestos exposure** was pervasive; over **3,000 lawsuits** were brought against Bethlehem Steel for asbestos-related diseases (the company stopped using asbestos in 1974)
- **Uranium processing** at the Lackawanna, NY plant (1949–1952) exposed workers to radiation without protective equipment or disclosure of hazards[^14]

### Peak Employment and Workforce Demographics

**Employment peaks:**
- **WWII peak: ~300,000 employees** company-wide (180,000 in shipbuilding)
- **Postwar peak: 165,000** full-time employees in 1957
- **Bethlehem, PA plant: 31,000+** workers at peak
- **Sparrows Point (Baltimore): 33,000** workers at peak in the 1950s (30,000 steel + ~3,000–4,000 shipyard); shipyard alone peaked at **8,600 during WWII**
- By **1999**, Sparrows Point was down to **3,000 employees**

**Workforce composition:**
- Workers from **50+ ethnic groups** immigrated to Bethlehem between 1910 and 1920: Hungarians, Italians, Poles, Slovaks, Ukrainians, and later Latinos
- The company deliberately organized labor groups by language/ethnicity — ostensibly for efficiency, but also to **discourage cross-ethnic solidarity** and union organizing
- **Women** worked at Bethlehem Steel throughout its history — as nurses, clerical workers, cooks, and phone operators. During WWII, women entered factory floor positions. **After the war, they were promptly fired** in favor of returning male workers
- The **1974 Supreme Court consent decree** forced major steel companies to hire women into unionized production jobs, ending a century of gendered employment discrimination[^15][^16]

### The Human Side: Community and Identity

South Bethlehem was a quintessential company town. The steel plant was the gravitational center:
- **Population grew ~300%** as immigrants flooded in for steel jobs
- Bethlehem Steel entered the **real estate market**, building company housing and offering mortgages to employees
- Tight-knit ethnic neighborhoods formed around **churches** — one or more per ethnic group
- A stark class divide: affluent executives and investors on **Fountain Hill** lived in sharp contrast to immigrant workers in South Bethlehem
- There was genuine **civic pride** in being a steelworker; the identity persisted long after the plant closed
- The PBS documentary "Bethlehem Steel: The People Who Built America" captured oral histories of this culture[^17]

## Conflicting Views / Debates

1. **Was the ERP (company union) a sham or a genuine benefit?** Some historians argue the ERP delivered real gains (work-sharing, paid vacations) and gave workers a voice, even if limited. Others view it as a cynical tool to prevent independent organization. The truth is likely both — it offered real but circumscribed benefits while serving management's strategic interest in avoiding CIO unions.

2. **Were high steelworker wages a cause or a symptom of decline?** Management blamed labor costs for uncompetitiveness. Labor advocates note that management failed to invest in modern technology (continuous casting, basic oxygen furnaces) and that executive compensation was equally lavish. The 60% wage premium over average manufacturing was real, but so was management's failure to modernize.

3. **The pension question:** Was the pension crisis caused by overgenerous promises to workers, or by management's failure to fund those promises? Grace's decision not to adequately fund pensions in the 1950s — when the company was highly profitable — suggests management bears significant responsibility.

4. **The role of ethnic division:** The company's practice of organizing workers by ethnicity is viewed by some as practical accommodation and by others as deliberate divide-and-rule strategy. The historical record supports the latter interpretation — ethnic segmentation made cross-group organizing harder and was advantageous to management.

2. **What was the racial composition of the workforce over time?** The 1974 consent decree suggests significant racial discrimination, but specific demographic breakdowns by race are scarce in available sources.

3. **How did Bethlehem Steel's safety record compare quantitatively to other major steelmakers?** The 500+ deaths figure (1905–1941) is striking, but comparable data from U.S. Steel or Republic Steel would provide context.

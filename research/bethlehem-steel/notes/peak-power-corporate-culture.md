# Peak Power & Corporate Culture (1920s–1960s)

## Summary

Bethlehem Steel's period of peak power spans roughly four decades, from its aggressive 1920s consolidation under Schwab and Grace through its postwar zenith in the late 1950s. The company became the second-largest steelmaker in the United States and, at times, the world — building the ships that won WWII and the structural steel that raised America's skyline. But beneath the production triumphs lay a deeply insular corporate culture defined by lavish executive compensation, anti-union hostility, and strategic complacency. The very management practices that drove growth — the bonus system, centralized authority under Grace, vertical integration — became liabilities as the world changed. By the early 1960s, at the very moment of peak output, the seeds of decline were already planted: failure to adopt basic oxygen furnaces and continuous casting, an inbred board that discouraged dissent, and a management class that lived in a bubble of country clubs and chauffeur-driven cars while foreign competitors rebuilt with superior technology.

## Key Findings

### Market Position and Scale

Bethlehem Steel was the **second-largest steelmaker in the United States** from the 1920s through its decline, trailing only U.S. Steel. After a series of aggressive acquisitions in the early 1920s — Lackawanna Steel ($60 million, 1922), Midvale Steel & Ordnance Company, and Cambria Steel Company (both 1923) — the company boosted its steelmaking capacity to **8.5 million tons** and employed over 60,000 people by the late 1920s.[^1][^2]

By the eve of WWII, Bethlehem operated **nine major steel plants** spread across the eastern United States. During the war, the company produced **73+ million tons of steel** and built **1,127 ships** for the U.S. military, with a wartime workforce exceeding **300,000** (180,000 in shipbuilding alone).[^3] In 1945, sales topped **$1.33 billion**.[^4]

The postwar peak came in **1957**: **19 million tons of steel produced**, with **$2.6 billion in sales** and **$190 million in earnings**. The full-time postwar employee roster stood at approximately **165,000** by decade's end.[^4]

### The Bethlehem Bonus — Origins and Scandal

Charles M. Schwab designed the bonus system as a performance incentive at every level. His philosophy: *"Do so much and you get so much; do more and you get more — that is the essence of the system."* Unlike U.S. Steel, which tied bonuses to overall company profitability, Schwab's system rewarded individual and unit performance — an approach that was genuinely innovative for its era.[^5]

The system worked brilliantly in motivating management, but it grew into an engine of executive excess. In **1929**, Eugene Grace received a bonus of **$1,623,753** (roughly $30 million in 2024 dollars) — equal to about **3.3% of company earnings**. The revelation stunned the nation; newspapers asked *"Is any man worth a million?"*[^6]

In early **1931**, stockholders sued Schwab and 12 other officers, demanding the return of **$36 million in bonuses distributed since 1911**. The settlement (July 1931) required future bonuses to be disclosed in annual reports and placed caps on total compensation.[^4] Schwab and Grace hired **Ivy Lee**, the father of public relations, to manage the fallout.[^7]

Despite the scandal, executive compensation at Bethlehem remained extraordinary. In **1941**, Grace received the **second-highest salary and compensation package in the U.S.** at $522,637.[^8] In **1958**, chairman Arthur B. Homer earned **$511,249** — the highest-paid corporate officer in America. In the mid-1950s, Bethlehem executives held **seven of the nation's ten highest-paid jobs**.[^9]

### Executive Culture — "Bonus Hill" and the Saucon Valley Bubble

Bethlehem Steel's executive culture was defined by extraordinary insularity and perquisites:

- **"Bonus Hill"**: Executives built mansions along **Prospect Avenue** in Bethlehem, a stretch nicknamed "Bonus Hill." Grace's mansion at **1317 Prospect Avenue** served as his home from 1923 to 1960.[^10]

- **The Elevator Ritual**: Two Bethlehem Steel police would watch for Grace's chauffeur-driven car. When spotted, a signal was sent to the office building. All elevators were stopped, occupants cleared, and the elevators returned to the lobby floor. Grace would select his elevator and ride alone in "splendid isolation" to his office.[^10]

- **Saucon Valley Country Club**: Founded in **1920** by 16 business leaders from Bethlehem Steel, the club grew to **850 acres** with multiple championship golf courses. Grace served as chairman of the Green's Committee from 1923 to 1960 and was its de facto ruler. One of the courses is named the "Grace Course" in his honor.[^11]

- **Corporate Dining Rooms**: Featured extravagant meals; leftovers were taken home by executives for private weekend entertaining.[^9]

- **Company-Paid Services**: Executives on Bonus Hill had domestic help and grounds staff paid for by the company.[^9]

- **Fleet of Perks**: The company maintained limousines with drivers and a fleet of private jet airplanes for executive use (only eliminated in the 1980s under Trautlein's cost-cutting).[^4]

### Leadership After Schwab

| Leader | Role | Tenure | Key Notes |
|--------|------|--------|-----------|
| **Charles M. Schwab** | Chairman | 1904–1939 (d. 1939) | Founded the company; designed bonus system; entrepreneurial visionary |
| **Eugene G. Grace** | President, then Chairman | President 1916–1945; Chairman 1945–1957 | Built Bethlehem into #2 steelmaker; anti-union; controlled corporate culture for 40+ years |
| **Arthur B. Homer** | President, then CEO | President 1945; CEO 1957–1964 | Highest-paid executive in U.S. (1958); oversaw wartime shipbuilding (1,127 ships); presided during peak production |
| **Edmund F. Martin** | President | 1960–1964 | First "Looper" (internal training program graduate) to lead the company; Stevens Tech graduate |

Grace's four-decade dominance shaped every aspect of the company. His anti-union stance set labor policy; his insularity defined the board; his conviction that steel demand would grow forever set strategic direction. As he put it: *"I have no doubt that the story will be one of increasing per capita use of steel"* and *"I have no qualms about excess capacity."*[^9]

### Strategic Decisions — Vertical Integration and Plant Investments

**Vertical integration** was a core strategy. Bethlehem controlled the entire supply chain: iron ore mines (domestic and in Cuba, South America, and Africa), coal mines, limestone quarries, oceanic transportation, and shipbuilding. The Bethlehem Shipbuilding Corporation, consolidated between 1905 and 1917 from numerous acquisitions including Fore River Shipbuilding Company (1913), became **America's largest shipbuilding enterprise**.[^12]

**Major facility investments:**

| Facility | Location | Peak Capacity / Employment | Notes |
|----------|----------|---------------------------|-------|
| **Sparrows Point** | Baltimore, MD | 8.2M short tons/yr; 30,000 workers | Largest steel mill in the world in the mid-1950s; 10 blast furnaces; 4 miles end to end |
| **Lackawanna** | Lackawanna, NY | 5.72M net tons/yr; 21,000 workers | Acquired 1922; third-largest steel producer globally at peak; $40M modernization in 1920s |
| **Burns Harbor** | Burns Harbor, IN | 5M tons/yr raw steel | Built 1962–1964; last large integrated steel plant built from scratch in the U.S.; over 50% of revenues by 1990s |
| **Bethlehem Plant** | Bethlehem, PA | Major structural steel hub | Produced wide-flange beams for skyscrapers; Grey mill (1908) was foundational |
| **Johnstown (Cambria)** | Johnstown, PA | Major rail/railcar production | Acquired via Midvale/Cambria (1923); rail production through 1991 |

The **Burns Harbor decision** (1962–1964) was perhaps the last major strategic investment — a $275 million commitment that positioned Bethlehem in the Midwest market. However, the company also invested heavily in "Big L," a massive blast furnace at Sparrows Point, which came online just as the company was hemorrhaging money.[^4]

### Signs of Complacency and Strategic Blindness

Even at peak power, critical warning signs were visible:

1. **Technology rejection**: Beginning in the 1950s, Japan and Germany rebuilt war-damaged plants with **basic oxygen furnaces (BOF)** and **continuous casting** — technologies that produced higher-quality steel at substantially lower cost. McLouth Steel adopted BOF in the U.S. in 1954. Bethlehem didn't introduce oxygen steelmaking until **1964**, a full decade later. Continuous casting was tried and **abandoned twice in separate experiments** in the 1960s.[^13][^9]

2. **Failed R&D investment**: Bethlehem built a **$100+ million research center** atop South Mountain in the 1960s, but it produced few usable innovations. Senior metallurgist Mike Herasimchuk observed: *"We were making evolutionary changes when a technological revolution was going on."*[^9]

3. **Inbred board**: Directors were exclusively internal company officers who socialized together constantly — lunching in corporate dining rooms, golfing at Saucon Valley. As one journalist observed, top executives *"rarely encountered a fresh or opposite point of view."* Community involvement was actively discouraged until the early 1970s.[^9]

4. **1959 steel strike**: The 116-day strike proved transformative for the industry. Imports jumped from **2 million tons (1958) to 5 million tons (1959)** and never returned to pre-strike levels. Customers discovered foreign steel was cheaper and often better — yet Bethlehem's management drew no strategic lessons.[^9]

5. **Confrontational labor relations**: Grace's anti-union legacy persisted. Rather than building cooperative relationships, management pursued adversarial bargaining that led to the disastrous **1973 Experimental Negotiating Agreement** (no-strike pact) — which locked in automatic wage escalation without productivity offsets. By 1982, average steelworker compensation reached **$26.29/hour plus fringes**, with 13-week sabbaticals every five years for senior workers.[^9]

6. **Import blindness**: In **1960**, U.S. steel imports exceeded exports for the first time in history. Bethlehem's leadership treated this as a temporary anomaly rather than a structural shift.[^4]

7. **No product research or innovation**: Throughout the 1960s and 1970s, company leaders believed procedures could continue as they had for over half a century without change. There was no meaningful effort in product research, process innovation, or organizational restructuring.[^4]

## Conflicting Views / Debates

**Was the bonus system innovative or corrupt?**
Schwab's defenders argue the bonus system was a genuinely progressive management innovation — a forerunner of performance-based compensation that aligned individual incentives with company goals. Critics (and the stockholders who sued) saw it as a self-dealing scheme that funneled corporate earnings to a small executive clique with minimal board oversight. The truth likely sits in between: it started as innovation and degenerated into entitlement.

**Was Grace a great leader or the architect of decline?**
Grace transformed Bethlehem from a minor producer to the world's second-largest steelmaker and led a historic wartime production effort. But his 40-year dominance also created the insular, change-resistant culture that proved fatal. His statement on learning of WWII's outbreak — *"Gentlemen, we are going to make a lot of money"* — captures both his commercial acumen and his moral blind spots.[^8]

**Was vertical integration a strength or a trap?**
Vertical integration gave Bethlehem control over costs and supply chains — a genuine competitive advantage for decades. But it also made the company enormous and rigid, with massive fixed costs that could not be adjusted when the market turned. Mini-mills, with no such burdens, could undercut integrated producers by the 1970s.

**Were the executives aware of the danger?**
Some evidence suggests individual managers recognized the technology gap and import threat. The R&D investment on South Mountain was an attempt at response. The continuous casting experiments show awareness. But the institutional culture suppressed dissent and rewarded conformity, so warnings were ignored or overridden.

## Open Questions

1. **What were the specific financial returns from the vertical integration strategy?** We know Bethlehem owned mines in Cuba, Latin America, and Africa, but how profitable were these operations compared to purchasing ore on the open market?

2. **Why did Bethlehem abandon continuous casting twice in the 1960s?** The specific technical or managerial reasons for these failures are not well documented in available sources. Was it a technical problem or an organizational one?

3. **How did the bonus system evolve after the 1931 settlement?** Were the caps meaningful, or did compensation simply shift to other forms (salary, perks, deferred compensation)?

4. **What role did the "Looper" system play in management development?** Edmund Martin was the first Looper to lead the company — was this internal training pipeline a strength (deep operational knowledge) or a weakness (homogeneity of thought)?

5. **How did Bethlehem's board composition compare to peer companies?** Was the all-insider board unusual for its era, or was this common in mid-century industrial companies?

6. **What was the South Mountain research center's specific budget and output?** The $100M+ figure is cited, but what specific projects were pursued, and why did they fail to translate into commercial innovations?

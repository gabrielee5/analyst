# Bethlehem Steel

## Research Question

How did Bethlehem Steel rise to become the second-largest steel producer in the United States and a cornerstone of American industrial power, and what combination of strategic, economic, competitive, and structural forces led to its decline and ultimate bankruptcy in 2001? What lessons does Bethlehem Steel's arc offer about industrial competitiveness, corporate adaptation, and the fate of legacy industries?

## Scope

**In scope:**
- Founding and early history (1857–1900), including the role of key figures like Charles M. Schwab
- The company's role in major infrastructure (bridges, skyscrapers, naval vessels) and wartime production
- Peak operations, market position, and corporate strategy (1900–1970)
- Labor relations, union dynamics, and the human cost of steelmaking
- The forces of decline: foreign competition, minimills, management decisions, pension/healthcare obligations
- The bankruptcy (2001) and aftermath — what happened to the workers, the plants, the city of Bethlehem
- Bethlehem Steel as a case study in deindustrialization and the Rust Belt phenomenon

**Out of scope:**
- Exhaustive technical metallurgy (beyond what's needed for narrative context)
- Detailed comparison with every global steel producer
- Post-bankruptcy real estate redevelopment (SteelStacks, casino) beyond a brief mention

## Key Areas to Investigate

1. Founding, early innovation, and the Schwab era — how Bethlehem became a giant
2. The company's role in building America: iconic structures, ships, and wartime arsenals
3. Peak power and corporate culture — management, strategy, and the "Grey Mill" mentality
4. Labor relations: unions, strikes, wages, and working conditions
5. The decline: foreign competition, minimills, and strategic failures (1970s–1990s)
6. Financial collapse: pension obligations, legacy costs, and the 2001 bankruptcy
7. Legacy and lessons: Bethlehem Steel as a symbol of American deindustrialization

## Constraints

- Standard depth: 5–7 deep dives
- Target report length: 3,000–5,000 words
- Focus on analytical insight over pure narrative — what can we learn, not just what happened

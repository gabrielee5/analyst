# Historical Origins of Chinese Stock Markets (1860s-1990)

## Summary

China's stock markets have a history stretching back over 150 years, far predating the Communist era. Securities trading began in Shanghai's foreign concessions in the 1860s, formalized into Asia's preeminent exchange by the 1930s, and was extinguished by the Communist revolution in 1949. After four decades of planned economy with no public securities markets, Deng Xiaoping's reform era cautiously revived equity markets in the 1980s, culminating in the reopening of the Shanghai and Shenzhen stock exchanges in late 1990. The turbulent early years -- including the 1992 Shenzhen share-subscription riot -- forced Beijing to build the regulatory architecture (the CSRC) that governs China's capital markets today.

## Key Findings

### 1. Pre-Republican Origins (1860s-1911)

**First securities trading (1866):** The earliest evidence of organized securities trading in Shanghai dates to June 1866, when a list of thirteen companies -- including the Hong Kong & Shanghai Banking Corporation -- appeared under a "Shares and Stocks" column in a local English-language newspaper. Trading was conducted entirely among foreign merchants in the International Settlement.[^1]

**Shanghai Sharebrokers' Association (1891):** During a boom in mining shares, American, British, and French businessmen founded the Shanghai Sharebrokers' Association -- China's first formal stock exchange. It was headquartered in the International Settlement and traded primarily shares of foreign-registered companies (shipping firms, banks, utilities, mining ventures).[^1][^2]

**Incorporation as "Shanghai Stock Exchange" (1904):** The Association applied for company registration in Hong Kong under the Companies Ordinance and was renamed the Shanghai Stock Exchange. It remained a foreign-run institution operating under British commercial law.[^1]

**The Rubber Stock Bubble of 1910:** A speculative mania in rubber plantation stocks -- fueled by booming global demand for automobile tires -- produced one of China's first financial crises. Key data:[^3][^4]

- Total investment in rubber stocks reached approximately 60 million Tael, with Chinese investors holding 70-80% of the exposure.
- Price-to-book ratios on rubber shares reached 10-20x; premiums of 8-9x face value were common.
- Example: Senawang rubber shares surged from 630 Tael (February 1910) to 1,675 Tael (April 1910).
- When the U.S. imposed barriers on rubber imports and international rubber prices collapsed (from peak to six shillings), the bubble burst.
- On July 21, 1910, three native banks (qianzhuang) failed. By October 7, foreign banks refused promissory notes from 21 Chinese banks.
- 48 of Shanghai's 91 native banks (53%) went bankrupt, with losses totaling 19.33 million Tael.
- The financial panic spread to Beijing, Guangzhou, and Chongqing, contributing to the fiscal pressures and Railway Protection Movement that helped trigger the 1911 Xinhai Revolution.

### 2. Republican-Era Heyday (1920s-1930s)

**Chinese-run exchanges emerge (1920-1921):** Two new exchanges -- the Shanghai Securities & Commodities Exchange (1920) and the Shanghai Chinese Merchant Exchange (1921) -- began operations, finally giving Chinese nationals direct participation in exchange management. These merged with the foreign exchange in 1928-1929 under a unified Shanghai Stock Exchange.[^1][^2]

**Far East financial center:** By the 1930s, Shanghai had emerged as the financial center of the Far East. Both Chinese and foreign investors traded stocks, debentures, government bonds, and futures. Market indices were calculated weekly: Swan, Culbertson and Fritz published three indices covering 23 Rubber Shares, 16 General Shares, and Internal Bonds.[^5]

**Integration with global markets:** By 1937, the Shanghai Stock Exchange was integrated with the New York Stock Exchange, with orders transmitted via cable and radio in as little as sixteen minutes. It was the largest equities market in Asia.[^6]

### 3. War and Closure (1937-1949)

**Japanese occupation:** The Shanghai Stock Exchange operated under increasing strain after Japan's invasion of China proper in 1937. Operations came to an abrupt halt on December 8, 1941, when Japanese troops occupied the Shanghai International Settlement following Pearl Harbor.[^1]

**Brief postwar reopening:** The exchange resumed operations in 1946 under the Nationalist government, but hyperinflation and civil war made normal trading nearly impossible.

**Communist closure (1949):** After the founding of the People's Republic of China on October 1, 1949, the new government shuttered the Shanghai Stock Exchange, liquidating and nationalizing its assets over the following year. Small exchanges that had briefly operated in Beijing and Tianjin (1949-1952) were also closed as the planned economy took hold.[^6][^7]

### 4. The Mao Era: No Public Markets (1950s-1970s)

Under Mao Zedong's centrally planned economy, there were no securities markets of any kind. Key characteristics:[^8]

- By 1978, nearly three-fourths of industrial production came from centrally controlled state-owned enterprises (SOEs) operating under planned output targets.
- Private enterprises and foreign-invested firms were generally barred.
- Only 3% of commodity prices were determined by the market.
- All individual household farms were collectivized into large communes during the 1950s.
- The concept of tradable equity in enterprises was ideologically incompatible with Marxist-Leninist economics as practiced in China during this period.

### 5. Deng Xiaoping's Reforms and the Return of Securities (1980s)

**First share issuances:** The revival of equity markets began tentatively in the mid-1980s as part of Deng Xiaoping's "Reform and Opening Up" program:[^9][^10]

- **November 1984:** Shanghai Feile Acoustics (飞乐音响, later Feilo Acoustics, stock code 600651) became the first company in PRC history to publicly issue shares, approved by the People's Bank of China's Shanghai Branch. It issued 10,000 shares at a face value of 50 yuan each.
- **January 12, 1987:** Shanghai Vacuum Electronics Devices became the first large/medium state-owned enterprise to adopt the shareholding system, issuing 145,000 ordinary shares at 100 yuan face value.
- **September 26, 1986:** Yanzhong Industrial and Feile Acoustics became the first stocks traded over the counter at the Jing'an Securities Department of ICBC's Shanghai Trust Investment Company -- China's first post-1949 stock transactions.

**Shenzhen parallel development:**
- **May 1987:** Shenzhen Development Bank launched its IPO, becoming the first bank in China to allow individual shareholdings.
- **April 1, 1988:** Shenzhen Development Bank shares began trading over the counter at the Shenzhen Special Economic Zone Securities Company.

### 6. The 1990 Reopening: SSE and SZSE

**Shanghai Stock Exchange (SSE):**[^6][^10][^11]

- **Established:** November 26, 1990. First board meeting held that day; directors, supervisory board members, and general manager elected.
- **First general manager:** Wei Wenyuan, formerly director of the Financial Administration Department at the People's Bank of China's Shanghai Branch.
- **Operations commenced:** December 19, 1990, at the Peacock Lounge of the Pujiang Hotel (formerly the Astor House Hotel). Mayor Zhu Rongji (later PRC Premier) presided over the opening ceremony before 500 dignitaries.
- **Initial listings:** 30 securities, including eight A-shares (the famous "Old Eight Stocks" / 老八股) and 17 treasury bonds. One hundred red-waistcoated brokers operated at computer desks -- China's first national computerized stock exchange.
- **The "Old Eight Stocks" (Lao Ba Gu, 老八股):** Shanghai Feile Acoustics, Shanghai Yanzhong Industrial, Shanghai Vacuum Electronics Devices, Shanghai Shenhua Electric Industrial, Shanghai Feile Co. (a separate entity), Shanghai Aishi Electronics Equipment, Zhejiang Phoenix Chemical, and Shanghai Yuyuan Tourism & Commerce City.
- **Historical symbolism:** This was only the second stock exchange reopening in a communist state, following Hungary's Budapest exchange by six months. The choice of the Astor House Hotel -- a landmark of old Shanghai's foreign commercial district -- was deliberately symbolic.

**Shenzhen Stock Exchange (SZSE):**[^12][^13]

- **Trial operation:** December 1, 1990. Shenzhen officials unilaterally announced the conversion of the over-the-counter market to an open exchange on December 18, 1990 -- one day before Shanghai's official opening -- without prior sanction from Beijing.
- **Beijing's objection:** The central government initially forced Shenzhen to delay its formal launch; the brass plaque was covered with red cloth. Full operations began July 3, 1991.
- **The "Old Five Stocks" (Lao Wu Gu, 老五股):** Shenzhen Development Bank, Shenzhen Vanke (listed January 29, 1991 -- the second stock traded on SZSE), Shenzhen Jintian, Shenzhen Anda, and Shenzhen Yuanye.

**Early market dynamics (1990-1991):**
- By end of 1991, Shanghai had 15 listings and Shenzhen had 17 -- primarily collective enterprises rather than state-owned firms.
- Market capitalization was minuscule. Trading volumes were thin and prices relatively stable.

### 7. Deng's Southern Tour and the 1992 Explosion

**The Southern Tour (January 18 - February 21, 1992):** At age 87, Deng Xiaoping traveled to Shenzhen and other Special Economic Zones, delivering speeches that restarted stalled economic reform after the post-Tiananmen political chill.[^14][^15]

**Key Deng quote on stock markets:** "Are securities and the stock market good or bad? Do they entail any dangers? Are they peculiar to capitalism? Can socialism make use of them? We allow people to reserve their judgement, but we must try these things out. If, after one or two years of experimentation, they prove feasible, we can expand them. Otherwise, we can put a stop to them and be done with it."[^15]

**Economic rationale:** Deng recognized that post-1989 retrenchment had depleted government coffers, while 20% of annual state revenue was being used to subsidize over 60% of state-owned enterprises that could not turn a profit. The shareholding system offered a way to recapitalize SOEs through public equity markets rather than perpetual government subsidies.[^10]

**Market explosion:** Share prices skyrocketed after the Southern Tour. Notable examples:[^10]
- Shanghai Yuyuan Company: stock rose from 100 yuan to 4,329 yuan by mid-March 1992.
- Shanghai Special Steel Tubing Company (listed May 25, 1992): shares rose 471% on debut day, yielding an average windfall of 20,000 yuan per worker-shareholder.
- Shanghai Vacuum Electronics: B-share offering in December 1991 sold out within one day.

### 8. The "8-10" Shenzhen Incident (August 8-10, 1992)

The most serious early crisis of China's reborn stock markets.[^10][^16]

**Background:** Following the Southern Tour, the government announced a new share subscription lottery in Shenzhen for fourteen companies slated for IPO. Five million lottery tickets were printed at 100 yuan each ($20), with only 10% of applicants winning the right to purchase shares.

**Events:**
- **August 7-8:** Even before the official sale date of August 9, hundreds of thousands of people began queuing at 300 distribution centers across Shenzhen. Lines stretched for blocks; people waited overnight.
- **August 9:** Over one million people (some estimates say 500,000-1,000,000+) packed the streets. Gang fights broke out in queues. Officials barricaded bank entrances with filing cabinets as crowds grew unruly.
- **August 10:** When forms ran out and rumors of insider corruption spread, riots erupted. Police deployed electric cattle prods, bamboo canes, and leather belts. Authorities escalated to high-pressure water hoses, tear gas, and fired live rounds into the air. More than a dozen people were injured.
- **Corruption allegations:** Demonstrators accused police officers and officials of siphoning subscription forms for personal profit and selling them on the black market at markups. Protesters carried placards reading "Down With Corruption!" Shenzhen authorities later acknowledged "fraud did happen at some stations."

**Aftermath:**
- Mayor Zheng Liangyu visited injured parties and publicly apologized for mismanagement.
- Additional subscription forms were promised.
- Trading was temporarily halted as the Shenzhen index plunged.
- Beijing expressed continuing support for market development and instructed financial institutions to buy shares on the exchanges to stabilize prices.
- The government chose a measured response over harsh crackdown -- a deliberate contrast with the 1989 Beijing approach.

**Regulatory consequence -- the birth of the CSRC:** The Shenzhen riot convinced top leadership that an unregulated market posed existential political risks. In October 1992, the State Council announced establishment of the State Council Securities Committee (SCSC) and the China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC) -- the specialized regulatory body that governs Chinese capital markets to this day.[^16]

# Market Architecture and Regulatory Framework of China's Stock Markets

## Summary

China operates four stock exchanges -- three on the mainland (Shanghai, Shenzhen, Beijing) and one offshore (Hong Kong) -- forming a multi-tiered capital market system designed to serve companies at different stages of development. The market features a unique share-class system (A/B/H/Red Chip/P-Chip) that reflects the country's controlled capital account and historical restrictions on foreign investment. The China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC) oversees the entire system, having completed a landmark shift from approval-based to registration-based IPO listing in February 2023. As of end-2025, the A-share market comprises 5,469 listed companies with a combined market capitalization of RMB 123 trillion (USD 17.5 trillion), making it the world's second-largest equity market.

## Key Findings

### 1. Shanghai Stock Exchange (SSE)

- **Founded**: December 1990; re-established after closure in 1949
- **Total market capitalization**: Exceeds USD 8.9 trillion (February 2026), making it the world's third-largest exchange
- **Structure**:
  - **Main Board**: The traditional listing venue for large-cap companies, predominantly state-owned enterprises (SOEs). In 2025, the main board accounted for RMB 67.2 billion raised across 36 IPO deals.
  - **STAR Market (Science and Technology Innovation Board)**: Launched July 22, 2019, as Shanghai's equivalent of NASDAQ. Focuses on semiconductor, biomedical, and high-end equipment companies. As of July 2025, nearly 590 companies are listed with a combined market cap exceeding RMB 7 trillion (USD 976 billion). Composition includes 120+ integrated circuit companies, 110+ biomedicine firms, and 120+ high-end equipment manufacturers. The STAR Market was the first board to pilot the registration-based IPO system.

### 2. Shenzhen Stock Exchange (SZSE)

- **Founded**: December 1990
- **Total market capitalization**: Exceeds USD 5.11 trillion (February 2026), world's 9th-largest exchange
- **Structure**:
  - **Main Board**: Serves established mid-to-large-cap companies
  - **SME Board**: Launched May 2004 for small and medium enterprises (note: merged into the main board in April 2021 as part of registration reform)
  - **ChiNext**: Launched October 2009 as a NASDAQ-style board for high-growth, high-tech startups. As of early 2024, approximately 1,337 companies were listed. ChiNext was the second board (after STAR) to adopt registration-based IPOs, doing so in August 2020.

### 3. Beijing Stock Exchange (BSE)

- **Founded**: November 15, 2021 (upgraded from the former NEEQ Select tier of the National Equities Exchange and Quotations system)
- **Purpose**: Serve "specialized, refined, distinctive, and innovative" (zhuan jing te xin) SMEs
- **Listed companies**: 274 as of September 2025, with total market capitalization of RMB 922 billion
- **Composition**: ~80% SMEs; over half are national-level "little giant" enterprises; 82% are classified as high-tech enterprises
- **Industrial clusters**: High-end equipment, information technology, consumer services, chemicals and new materials, biomedicine
- **2025 performance**: BSE received 113 IPO applications in 2025 -- more than Shanghai and Shenzhen combined. The BSE 50 index rose 37.6% in 2025, vs. 2.5% for the CSI 300
- **Trading rules**: 30% daily price limit (wider than SSE/SZSE main boards); registration-based IPO system from inception

### 4. Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing (HKEX)

- **Main Board**: 2,352 listed companies as of November 2025; primary venue for large-cap listings
- **GEM (Growth Enterprise Market)**: 313 listed companies as of November 2025; serves smaller, growth-stage companies
- **Mainland China presence**: 1,447 mainland enterprises listed (as of end-2023), constituting ~55% of total listed companies and ~76.5% of total market capitalization
- **Role**: Primary offshore listing venue for mainland Chinese companies; gateway for international capital into China via Stock Connect (launched 2014 for Shanghai, 2016 for Shenzhen)

### 5. Share Class System

China's equity market features a complex share-class taxonomy reflecting capital account restrictions:

| Share Class | Currency | Listing Venue | Incorporation | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A-shares** | RMB | SSE / SZSE / BSE | Mainland China | Domestic shares; accessible to foreign investors via QFII, RQFII, and Stock Connect. 5,469 companies as of end-2025 |
| **B-shares** | USD (SSE) / HKD (SZSE) | SSE / SZSE | Mainland China | Legacy class for foreign investors; largely superseded by Stock Connect. B-shares often trade at 50% discount to A-share counterparts due to illiquidity |
| **H-shares** | HKD | HKEX | Mainland China | Mainland-incorporated companies listed in Hong Kong; typically large SOEs. Subject to both CSRC and HKEX regulation |
| **Red Chips** | HKD | HKEX | Offshore (Cayman, BVI, Bermuda) | Controlled by mainland state entities or provincial/municipal governments. Incorporated outside China but with mainland operations |
| **P-Chips** | HKD | HKEX | Offshore (Cayman, BVI, Bermuda) | Privately controlled mainland companies listed in Hong Kong. Run by private-sector Chinese entrepreneurs |

### 6. VIE (Variable Interest Entity) Structures

- **What**: A legal structure allowing foreign investors to gain economic exposure to Chinese companies in sectors restricted from foreign direct investment (telecom, internet, media, education)
- **How it works**: Foreign shareholders own equity in an offshore shell company (typically Cayman Islands-incorporated), which controls a Hong Kong subsidiary, which in turn establishes a wholly foreign-owned enterprise (WFOE) in mainland China. The WFOE uses a series of contractual arrangements (service agreements, licensing, exclusive option agreements) to control and consolidate the domestic operating entity without owning equity in it
- **Scale**: As of July 2021, 196 Chinese companies using VIE structures were listed on US exchanges, representing 68.8% of all US-listed Chinese companies. Major examples include Alibaba, Tencent, JD.com, Baidu, and Pinduoduo
- **Regulatory status**: Chinese regulators have neither confirmed nor denied the general legality of VIEs. The CSRC examines them on a case-by-case basis. This legal ambiguity is itself a form of regulatory risk for foreign investors
- **Why it persists**: VIEs are tolerated because they channel foreign capital into China's tech sector. Banning them retroactively would destabilize hundreds of billions in market capitalization

### 7. CSRC as Regulator

The China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC) is the primary regulator of China's securities and futures markets, operating under the State Council.

**Key powers**:
- Drafting securities laws and regulations
- Supervising stock and futures exchanges
- Approving (now registering) securities issuance
- Regulating listed companies, brokerages, fund managers, and market intermediaries
- Enforcement against insider trading, market manipulation, and fraud

**Registration-Based IPO Reform** (landmark change):
- **Old system**: Approval-based; CSRC had substantial discretion to withhold approval even if issuers met all requirements. Created bottlenecks and rent-seeking
- **Pilot**: Registration system first piloted on the STAR Market (July 2019), then ChiNext (August 2020), then BSE (November 2021)
- **Full implementation**: February 17, 2023, when the CSRC officially extended registration-based IPOs to the main boards of both SSE and SZSE
- **How it works now**: Vetting power transferred to the exchanges themselves; CSRC checks applications against national industrial policy and board positioning. Emphasis on disclosure rather than merit-based approval
- **October 2025 tightening**: CSRC introduced new measures to reform the offline IPO placement mechanism, giving larger allocations to institutions that commit to longer lock-up periods, aiming to reduce speculative trading in new listings

### 8. Trading Rules

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| **T+1 Trading** | Investors cannot sell shares bought on the same day. Buying shares sold earlier the same day is permitted. Securities settle T+0; cash settles T+1. In effect since the exchanges opened in 1990 |
| **Daily Price Limits (Main Boards)** | +/-10% of previous closing price for regular stocks; +/-5% for stocks on the risk warning (ST) board |
| **Daily Price Limits (ChiNext/STAR)** | +/-20% of previous closing price (expanded from 10% in August 2020 for ChiNext). No price limit for the first 5 trading days after IPO |
| **Daily Price Limits (BSE)** | +/-30% of previous closing price |
| **Circuit Breakers** | Introduced January 1, 2016, with a 5% trigger (15-min halt) and 7% trigger (market closure for the day) on the CSI 300 index. Triggered on January 4 and January 7, 2016, with the market shutting down after only 28 minutes of trading on Jan 7. Suspended permanently on January 8, 2016 -- just 4 trading days after implementation. Widely criticized as too restrictive (cf. US thresholds of 7%/13%/20% on the S&P 500) |
| **Short Selling** | Highly restricted. In January 2024, CSRC suspended lending of shares with lock-up restrictions for short selling. In March 2024, brokerages were banned from borrowing shares for lending to short-sellers, and same-day short selling of borrowed stock was banned. In July 2024, securities relending was fully suspended and margin ratios increased (80% to 100% for general; 100% to 120% for private funds). By mid-2024, outstanding short-selling positions had fallen 64% and securities lending by 75% |
| **Trading Hours** | 9:30-11:30 AM, 1:00-3:00 PM (Beijing time), Monday-Friday. Pre-opening call auction: 9:15-9:25 AM |
| **Lot Size** | 100 shares (1 board lot) for A-shares |

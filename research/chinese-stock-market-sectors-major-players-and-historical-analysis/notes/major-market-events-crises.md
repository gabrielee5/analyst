# Major Market Events and Crises: China's Stock Market (2005--2026)

## Summary

China's stock market history from 2005 to 2026 is a story of extraordinary boom-bust cycles driven by the interplay of policy, leverage, geopolitics, and retail speculation. The SSE Composite has oscillated between ~998 (June 2005) and 6,124 (October 2007), crashed 72% during the GFC, inflated another leveraged bubble to 5,178 (June 2015), endured a multi-year bear market from 2021--2024 triggered by tech crackdowns, property defaults, and COVID lockdowns, and then staged a dramatic recovery catalyzed by the September 2024 stimulus bazooka and the January 2025 DeepSeek AI rally. As of April 2026, the SSE Composite trades around 3,949 and the CSI 300 near 4,688, with consensus expecting ~14--15% earnings growth amid continued policy support and AI-driven optimism.

## 1. The Split-Share Reform Bull Run (2005--2007)

### The Non-Tradable Share Problem

Before 2005, approximately two-thirds of all shares outstanding on China's exchanges were non-tradable (NTS). These were held by the state, state-owned enterprises, and legal persons. Only the remaining one-third---"tradable shares"---could be bought and sold by public investors. This created a fundamental structural distortion: the controlling shareholders (the state) had no market-linked incentive to maximize shareholder value because their shares could not be sold. It also suppressed market capitalization, limited liquidity, and discouraged institutional participation.

### How the Reform Worked

In April 2005, the CSRC launched pilot batches of firms to undergo reform. The mechanism required holders of non-tradable shares to compensate holders of tradable shares (typically via bonus shares, warrants, or cash payments) in exchange for the right to eventually sell their shares on the open market. The compensation was negotiated firm-by-firm between tradable and non-tradable shareholders.

- **April--June 2005**: Two pilot batches initiated
- **August 2005**: Reform expanded to all listed firms
- **September 2005--January 2007**: SSE and SZSE published weekly batches of reforming firms
- **End of 2007**: 1,254 firms completed the reform, representing over 97% of total market capitalization

### The Bull Run

- **June 6, 2005**: SSE Composite hit a low of approximately 998, marking the market bottom
- **October 16, 2007**: SSE Composite peaked at 6,124 --- a roughly 514% gain in 28 months
- The Shanghai market rose more than six-fold from trough to peak

### What Drove Retail Mania

- **New accounts**: The number of stock trading accounts in China exceeded 100 million in May 2007 for the first time
- **Behavioral shift**: A June 2007 PBOC survey found that over 40% of Chinese residents favored stock and fund investment, surpassing savings deposits as the preferred investment vehicle for the first time
- **P/E ratios**: Valuations reached extreme levels; the SSE traded at P/E ratios above 70x at the peak
- **Catalysts**: The split-share reform unlocked latent value and signaled government commitment to market development. RMB appreciation expectations and massive liquidity growth (M2 expanding rapidly) further fueled inflows

## 2. The 2008 Global Financial Crisis

### The Crash

- **October 16, 2007**: SSE Composite peaked at 6,124
- **October 28, 2008**: SSE Composite bottomed at approximately 1,664
- **Decline**: ~72.8% from peak to trough in roughly 12 months
- This was one of the largest percentage declines of any major global index during the GFC

The crash was triggered by the bursting of the domestic bubble (extreme valuations, monetary tightening in 2007--2008), compounded by the global financial crisis and collapse in export demand.

### China's 4 Trillion RMB Stimulus

- **November 9, 2008**: The State Council announced a RMB 4 trillion (US$586 billion) stimulus package
- **Breakdown**:
  - Public infrastructure (railways, roads, airports): RMB 1.5 trillion (38%)
  - Sichuan earthquake reconstruction: RMB 1 trillion
  - Rural development and technology: RMB 370 billion each
  - Environmental/energy initiatives: RMB 210 billion
  - Education, culture, social welfare: RMB 150 billion

### Recovery Speed

- China's GDP growth fell to nearly 6% by end of 2008
- By mid-2009, growth had recovered to over 10%
- Economic expansion continued at approximately 10% through 2010
- The SSE Composite recovered to ~3,400 by August 2009 (roughly doubling from the October 2008 low)

### Aftermath and Criticism

- The stimulus was credited with making China the first major economy to recover
- Critics blamed it for a massive surge in local government and SOE debt
- Estimates suggested as much as 20% of stimulus-related loans would eventually be written off
- It planted the seeds for subsequent property-sector leverage that would explode in 2021--2023

## 3. The 2014--2015 Leveraged Bubble and Crash

### The Bubble

- **Mid-2014**: SSE Composite traded around 2,000
- **June 12, 2015**: SSE Composite peaked at 5,178 --- a ~150% gain in roughly one year
- The rally was overwhelmingly driven by **margin trading**: outstanding margin loans surged from RMB 698 billion to RMB 2.7 trillion
- Retail investors piled in using borrowed money, often through unregulated shadow lending channels beyond official brokerage margin accounts
- The government had actively encouraged equity market participation as an alternative to the slowing real estate market

### The Crash

- **June 12--July 8, 2015**: SSE fell ~30% in three weeks
- **July 8, 2015**: Over 1,400 companies (more than half of all listed firms) filed trading halts to prevent further declines
- **August 24, 2015** ("Black Monday"): SSE dropped 8.49%, the largest single-day fall since 2007
- **August 25, 2015** ("Black Tuesday"): SSE fell a further 7%+
- A third of the value of A-shares was lost within one month
- Total global value destruction estimated at US$4 trillion among companies reliant on Chinese markets

### The "National Team" Intervention

The government organized an unprecedented rescue operation through state-owned financial institutions collectively dubbed the "national team":

- **China Securities Finance Corporation (CSFC)** and **Central Huijin Investment** were the primary vehicles
- The national team spent approximately **RMB 1.6 trillion** purchasing shares of more than 1,000 firms
- Additional measures included:
  - Suspension of all new IPOs
  - Restrictions on short selling and net selling by brokerages
  - Cash provisions to brokers backed by central bank reserves
  - Six-month ban on any shareholder owning 5%+ of a company from selling
  - Arrest of 197 people for "spreading market rumors"

### The January 2016 Circuit Breaker Fiasco

- **January 1, 2016**: China introduced a market-wide circuit breaker mechanism
  - 5% decline: 15-minute trading halt
  - 7% decline: trading suspended for the rest of the day
- **January 4, 2016** (first trading day): Circuit breaker triggered as the market fell ~7%
- **January 7, 2016**: Market hit the 7% limit within 27 minutes of opening; trading halted for the entire day
- **January 7, 2016 (evening)**: CSRC suspended the circuit breaker mechanism after just 4 days
- The circuit breakers had created a powerful "magnet effect" --- as the index approached the 5% threshold, panic selling accelerated as traders rushed to exit before being locked in
- By end of 2015, the SSE Composite was still up 12.6% for the year but was 50% below June highs
- The market stabilized around 3,000 by January 2017

## 4. The 2018 US-China Trade War

### Timeline of Escalation

- **March 8, 2018**: US imposed 25% tariffs on Chinese steel imports and 10% on aluminum
- **March 22, 2018**: Trump signed a memorandum targeting $50 billion in Chinese goods, citing Section 301 (intellectual property theft)
- **June 2018**: $50 billion of Chinese exports hit with 25% tariffs (two tranches)
- **July 2018**: China retaliated with tariffs on an equal amount of US goods (crude oil, LNG, medical equipment, agricultural products)
- **September 2018**: US imposed 10% tariffs on an additional $200 billion of Chinese goods
- **December 1, 2018**: At the G20 in Buenos Aires, Trump and Xi agreed to a 90-day trade truce
- **December 1, 2018** (same day): Huawei CFO Meng Wanzhou arrested in Vancouver at US request, threatening to derail the ceasefire

### Market Impact

- **SSE Composite**: Fell from ~3,500 (January 2018) to ~2,449 (December 2018) --- a decline of approximately 28--30%
- **MSCI China A Index**: Fell 30.16% for full-year 2018
- **Dow Jones**: Dropped 4.7% in the days following initial tariff announcements
- Asian markets fell 2.5--3% on the Meng Wanzhou arrest news

### Tech Sector and Huawei

- The arrest of Meng Wanzhou on December 1, 2018 was a watershed moment, signaling that the trade war extended beyond tariffs into technology and national security
- US export controls began targeting Huawei and ZTE
- US companies with heavy China exposure (Apple, semiconductor firms, Boeing) faced significant stock pressure
- The Huawei sanctions would escalate further in 2019--2020, eventually cutting off Huawei from TSMC chip fabrication

### Recovery

- The MSCI China A Index rose 36.40% in 2019 as trade tensions de-escalated toward a Phase One deal
- The SSE Composite recovered to ~3,000 by early 2019

## 5. The 2020 COVID Crash and Recovery

### The Crash

- **January 23, 2020**: China extended the Lunar New Year holiday as COVID-19 spread from Wuhan
- **February 3, 2020** (first trading day after extended holiday): SSE Composite fell **7.7%**, shedding ~$375 billion in market value in a single session --- the steepest one-day decline since August 2015
- **CSI 300**: Fell 7.88% on the same day
- **Shenzhen Composite**: Fell 8.4%
- The PBOC injected RMB 1.2 trillion in liquidity via reverse repos on that day to stabilize the market

### The V-Shaped Recovery

- China's aggressive containment measures (Wuhan lockdown, mass testing) brought case counts under control by March 2020
- Stock prices recovered roughly half their losses between March 23 and April 9, 2020
- By mid-2020, the CSI 300 had surpassed its pre-COVID levels
- **February 18, 2021**: CSI 300 reached an all-time high of 5,807.72

### Why China Recovered Faster

1. **Early and aggressive containment**: China locked down Wuhan on January 23, 2020 and implemented mass testing infrastructure ahead of most countries
2. **Industrial production rebound**: Factories reopened by late February/March 2020; China was back to positive GDP growth by Q2 2020
3. **Policy support**: PBOC cut rates and injected liquidity; fiscal measures targeted infrastructure and SMEs
4. **Export boom**: As the rest of the world locked down, demand for Chinese-manufactured goods (PPE, electronics for remote work) surged
5. **Limited financial-sector damage**: Unlike 2008, there was no systemic banking crisis; the shock was real-economy-focused

## 6. The 2020--2021 Tech Regulatory Crackdown

### The Trigger: Jack Ma's Speech and Ant Group

- **October 24, 2020**: Jack Ma delivered a provocative speech at the Bund Finance Summit in Shanghai. He criticized the Basel Accords as outdated, said China's regulators focused too much on "preventing systemic risk" at the expense of innovation, and suggested they were stifling opportunities for young people and developing countries.
- **November 2, 2020**: Ma, along with Ant Group Chairman Eric Jing and CEO Simon Hu, were summoned for a yuetan ("regulatory interview" / dressing-down) by the PBOC, CBIRC, CSRC, and SAFE
- **November 3, 2020**: Shanghai Stock Exchange suspended Ant Group's IPO just 36 hours before its scheduled listing. The IPO would have raised US$34.5 billion, valuing Ant at US$313 billion --- it would have been the world's largest IPO
- Jack Ma effectively disappeared from public view for several months

### The Regulatory Cascade (2021)

The Ant Group suspension was just the opening salvo in a 32-month crackdown:

- **December 2020**: State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR) launched antitrust investigation into Alibaba
- **March 2021**: SAMR fined 12 tech companies for anti-monopoly violations
- **April 2021**: Alibaba fined a record **RMB 18.2 billion (US$2.8 billion)** for monopolistic practices; 34 additional tech firms warned
- **June 30, 2021**: Didi Chuxing IPO'd on the NYSE at a US$70 billion valuation
- **July 2, 2021**: CAC launched a cybersecurity investigation into Didi just two days after its IPO; app pulled from Chinese app stores
- **July 2021**: "Double Reduction" policy banned for-profit tutoring in core school subjects, effectively destroying the ~$100 billion private education industry overnight. Companies like New Oriental, TAL Education, and Gaotu collapsed 70--90%
- **August 2021**: Gaming restrictions imposed --- minors limited to 3 hours per week of online gaming (Friday, Saturday, Sunday only)
- **Didi**: Eventually forced to delist from NYSE (May 2022); fined US$1.2 billion. Market cap collapsed from $70 billion to ~$16.7 billion

### Value Destruction

- **KWEB ETF** (KraneShares CSI China Internet): Lost 48.9% in 2021 and 17.2% in 2022. From its February 2021 peak of ~$103, it lost more than three-quarters of its value
- **Ant Group**: Valued at US$313 billion pre-IPO; by July 2023, its buyback implied a valuation 70% lower (~$94 billion)
- **Aggregate**: The crackdown erased an estimated **$1.5 trillion** from Chinese tech stocks at its most extreme
- The Hang Seng Tech Index fell from ~11,000 (February 2021) to under 3,000 (October 2022)

## 7. The 2022--2023 Property Crisis and Zero-COVID Lockdowns

### The Three Red Lines

In **August 2020**, Chinese regulators introduced the "Three Red Lines" policy --- financial thresholds for property developers:

1. Liability-to-asset ratio below 70%
2. Net gearing ratio (debt/equity) below 100%
3. Cash-to-short-term-borrowing ratio above 1.0x

Developers who breached all three lines (as Evergrande did) were prohibited from increasing their debt. This triggered a liquidity squeeze across the sector.

### Evergrande Default Timeline

- **September 2021**: Evergrande began missing interest payments on wealth management products and supplier payments
- **October 2021**: Evergrande narrowly avoided formal default by making an $83.5 million bond coupon payment within the grace period
- **December 7, 2021**: Evergrande missed a deadline for interest payments on US dollar bonds at the end of a 30-day grace period
- **December 9, 2021**: Fitch downgraded Evergrande from "C" to "RD" (Restricted Default), formally declaring default on offshore bonds
- **July 2023**: Evergrande disclosed net losses of RMB 476 billion (US$66.3 billion) for 2021 and RMB 105.9 billion (US$14.7 billion) for 2022
- **Total liabilities**: RMB 2.43 trillion (~US$340 billion) as of December 31, 2022
- **January 2024**: Hong Kong court ordered Evergrande's liquidation

### Country Garden Crisis

- Country Garden was long considered a "model developer" --- more conservative and better managed than Evergrande
- **August 2023**: Country Garden warned it was on the "brink" of default
- **October 10, 2023**: Country Garden failed to pay HK$470 million (US$60 million) in principal when due
- **October 25, 2023**: Formally declared in default on a dollar bond (first time)
- **First-half 2023 loss**: US$7 billion --- a record for the company
- Country Garden's collapse was arguably worse for confidence than Evergrande's because it shattered the belief that the crisis was limited to over-leveraged outliers

### Zero-COVID Lockdowns

- **March--June 2022**: Shanghai locked down for two months, disrupting supply chains and consumer activity
- **November 2022**: Widespread protests ("White Paper Revolution") against Zero-COVID
- **December 7, 2022**: China abruptly abandoned Zero-COVID, triggering a massive but chaotic reopening wave
- Lockdowns suppressed property sales, stalled construction, and crushed consumer confidence

### Market Impact and Foreign Investor Exodus

- **SSE Composite**: Fell to approximately 2,885 in October 2022; then further to ~2,635 in January/February 2024
- **CSI 300**: Hit a five-year low in early February 2024
- **Foreign investor outflows**: Net FDI plummeted from peak inflows of $334 billion (2021) to outflows of nearly $154 billion (2024)
- Northbound Stock Connect: Foreign investors withdrew RMB 90 billion in August 2023 --- the largest monthly outflow since the program launched in 2014
- Full-year 2023: Overseas investors recorded their smallest-ever annual net purchases of Chinese stocks via Stock Connect --- just RMB 44 billion (US$6.1 billion)
- The **"China uninvestable" narrative** took hold among global allocators; many active managers with long-term horizons excluded China from strategic asset allocation entirely

### February 2024 Intervention

- In early February 2024, the head of the CSRC was dismissed and replaced by Wu Qing, known as the "Butcher Broker" for his tough regulatory record
- The national team aggressively purchased ETFs to stabilize large-cap stocks
- Quant funds were targeted with restrictions after being blamed for exacerbating small-cap selling
- The CSI 300 rose in nine consecutive sessions (February 19--23, 2024) --- the longest uninterrupted rally in six years

## 8. The September 2024 Stimulus Bazooka

### The Announcement (September 24, 2024)

PBOC Governor Pan Gongsheng held a rare press conference and unveiled a coordinated stimulus package:

1. **RRR cut**: 0.5 percentage points, injecting ~RMB 1 trillion (US$137 billion) in liquidity
2. **Seven-day reverse repo rate cut**: 20 basis points to 1.5%
3. **Loan prime rates and deposit rates**: Both reduced
4. **Existing mortgage rates**: Cut by ~0.5 percentage points, saving homeowners an estimated RMB 150 billion (US$21.1 billion) in annual interest
5. **Stock market support tools**: PBOC created a swap facility allowing securities firms, funds, and insurers to pledge assets for PBOC liquidity to buy stocks; also a re-lending facility for companies to conduct share buybacks

### The Rally

- **CSI 300**: Surged approximately **25--30%** in the three weeks following the announcement
- **September 24**: CSI 300 jumped 4.3% on the announcement day alone
- **September 30** (last trading day before Golden Week): Markets surged on FOMO as investors rushed to buy before the week-long holiday
- **October 8** (first day after Golden Week): CSI 300 opened up ~10%, with turnover exceeding RMB 3.4 trillion --- a record for A-shares

### The Hangover

- **October 8--9**: After the initial surge, the National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC) press conference on October 8 disappointed investors by announcing no major new fiscal stimulus
- Markets began pulling back from peak euphoria
- The rally lost momentum as investors realized the measures were primarily monetary (liquidity-focused) rather than fiscal (demand-stimulating)
- By late October/November, the CSI 300 had given back a portion of gains but remained ~15--20% above pre-stimulus levels
- The Hang Seng Index fell 6.5% in the days following the NDRC disappointment

### Assessment

The stimulus marked a clear policy inflection point --- Beijing acknowledged the severity of the economic slowdown and signaled willingness to act aggressively. However, the absence of large-scale fiscal spending (consumer vouchers, local government debt resolution) left the fundamental demand problem unresolved. The episode demonstrated that China's market could still generate violent short-term rallies on policy signals, but sustained recovery required follow-through.

## 9. The 2025 DeepSeek AI Rally

### The Catalyst

- **January 20, 2025**: DeepSeek released its R1 reasoning model
- By **January 27**, DeepSeek surpassed ChatGPT as the most downloaded free app on the US iOS App Store
- DeepSeek-R1 reportedly achieved performance comparable to OpenAI's models at a fraction of the training cost (estimated $5.6 million vs. hundreds of millions for frontier US models)
- The release triggered an 18% drop in Nvidia's share price as investors questioned whether massive GPU capex was necessary

### The Rally

- **Hang Seng Tech Index**: Surged 28.5% from January 20 through early March 2025; finished 2025 up ~23--24%
- **Hang Seng Index**: Gained 20.5% over the same period; advanced nearly 30% for the year
- **Alibaba**: Shares jumped over 60% year-to-date by mid-2025
- **Tencent**: Climbed more than 33%, hitting its highest level in nearly three years
- Record-breaking Southbound capital inflows (mainland investors buying Hong Kong stocks) further fueled the rally

### The "China Tech Is Back" Narrative

Several factors converged to reshape the narrative:

1. **DeepSeek proved Chinese AI competitiveness**: The model demonstrated that China could produce frontier AI despite US chip export restrictions, challenging the assumption that sanctions would cripple Chinese tech
2. **Xi Jinping's February 17, 2025 meeting**: Xi convened a rare high-profile symposium with CEOs from Huawei, Alibaba, Tencent, Xiaomi, BYD, CATL, and DeepSeek's founder. Jack Ma attended publicly for the first time since the 2020 crackdown --- a powerful symbolic signal that the tech crackdown era was over
3. **Policy pivot**: Beijing signaled greater support for the private sector and technological self-reliance, contrasting sharply with the 2021--2023 regulatory hostility
4. **Valuation gap**: After three years of crackdown-driven de-rating, Chinese tech stocks traded at massive discounts to US peers, creating a compelling re-rating opportunity

### Hong Kong Market Outperformance

Hong Kong's market, particularly the Hang Seng Tech Index, dramatically outperformed mainland A-shares in 2025. This was driven by:
- Greater concentration of tech/internet names in Hong Kong listings
- Easier access for global funds (no QFII restrictions)
- Southbound flows from mainland investors seeking tech exposure
- IPO market revival, with Chinese tech companies choosing Hong Kong over US listings

## 10. 2025--2026 Current State

### Market Levels (as of April 1, 2026)

- **SSE Composite**: ~3,949 (up 1.46% on the day)
- **CSI 300**: ~4,688 (up ~15% over the past 12 months)
- **Hang Seng Index**: Advanced nearly 30% in 2025; continued positive momentum into early 2026
- **Hang Seng Tech Index**: Up 23% in 2025; further gains in early 2026 driven by AI chip IPO surge

### Tariff and Trade Situation

- Trump tariffs on China remain at approximately 47%, well above the 20.7% pre-inauguration level
- A one-year trade truce was agreed when Xi and Trump met in South Korea in 2025
- Exports to the US fell 17.7% YoY through October 2025
- **April 2026**: Trump is scheduled to visit China, an opportunity to broaden the trade truce and shift the geopolitical narrative
- Despite tariffs, Chinese markets have shown resilience --- the CSI 300 did not collapse on tariff escalation, reflecting market maturity and domestic-demand orientation

### Policy Stance

- **PBOC**: Remains on an easing trajectory; accommodative monetary policy expected through 2026
- **Fiscal**: Sustained fiscal expansion, with local government special bond issuance supporting infrastructure
- **Capital markets**: SASAC introduced stock price performance as a KPI for SOE managers, driving A-share dividend payout ratios to ~35% in 2025 (up from 30% in 2024), with SOEs approaching 40%
- **Anti-involution agenda**: Policy discourages ruinous price wars (solar, EVs, consumer goods), engineering margin recovery across industries
- **15th Five-Year Plan**: Focus on technological self-reliance, domestic consumption, and high-quality development

### Key Themes for 2026

1. **AI and tech leadership**: DeepSeek catalyzed a structural re-rating of Chinese tech; continued AI model releases and chip self-sufficiency efforts sustain the narrative
2. **Earnings acceleration**: Consensus expects CSI 300 earnings growth of ~14--15% in 2026, a meaningful pickup from muted post-reopening years
3. **Valuation**: CSI 300 trades at ~12.8x forward earnings vs. 14.2x five-year average --- mid-range by historical standards, attractive relative to global peers
4. **Foreign investor re-engagement**: The "uninvestable" label is fading; China's $19 trillion stock market is drawing foreign inflows again after a three-year drought
5. **Property stabilization**: While the property sector remains a drag, policy measures (mortgage rate cuts, purchase restriction removals) are gradually improving sales volumes in tier-1 cities
6. **Geopolitical risk premium**: US-China tensions remain the key tail risk; tariff escalation or tech sanctions could disrupt the recovery narrative

# Major Players: Financials, Energy, and Industrials Sectors

**Research date:** April 2026

---

## Summary

China's Financials, Energy, and Industrials sectors are dominated by massive state-owned enterprises (SOEs) that rank among the world's largest companies by assets and revenue, yet frequently trade at steep valuation discounts to global peers. The "Big Four" state banks alone hold over $15 trillion in combined assets, making them the largest banks on Earth by that measure, while trading at average P/B ratios around 0.6x. In Energy, the three national oil companies (PetroChina, Sinopec, CNOOC) coexist with a fast-growing clean energy ecosystem led by firms like CATL (39% global EV battery share), LONGi Green Energy, and Yangtze Power. The Industrials sector features globally dominant players in EV batteries, rolling stock, and construction equipment, with CATL's ~$275 billion market cap making it the sector's standout.

### 1.4 Why Chinese Banks Trade at Low P/E and P/B Ratios

Despite being the largest banks in the world by assets, Chinese banks trade at persistently depressed valuations. As of 2025, the sector-average P/B ratio was approximately **0.61x** -- compared with 1.0-1.5x for major U.S. and European banks. Key reasons:

1. **Compressed net interest margins (NIMs):** The PBOC has aggressively cut lending rates (1-year LPR to 3.0%, 5-year LPR to 3.5% in 2025), squeezing bank profitability. ROA and ROE have fallen to record lows.

2. **Property sector exposure:** Multi-year real estate downturn has raised fears of large hidden non-performing loans (NPLs). Rising special-mention loans signal credit deterioration. Banks carry significant exposure to distressed developers and local government financing vehicles (LGFVs).

3. **LGFV and local government debt risks:** Local governments accumulated massive off-balance-sheet debt through financing vehicles. The implicit guarantee creates moral hazard and potential credit losses that are difficult to quantify.

4. **Policy-directed lending:** State banks are routinely directed to lend at below-market rates to support government policy objectives (infrastructure, SMEs, green transition), sacrificing profitability.

5. **Geopolitical discount:** U.S.-China tensions, tariff risks (renewed under Trump administration), and potential sanctions create an ongoing risk premium for foreign investors.

6. **Weak credit growth:** Economic slowdown has depressed loan demand, particularly from the private sector.

7. **Dividend yield as silver lining:** The low valuations produce attractive dividend yields (often 5-7%), which have drawn domestic institutional interest -- Chinese banks were "stock market darlings" in early 2025 as yield-seeking capital rotated into high-dividend SOEs.[^1]

### 2.4 Fossil Fuels vs. Clean Energy: The Central Tension

China's energy sector embodies a fundamental contradiction. The country is simultaneously the world's largest consumer of coal and the world's largest investor in clean energy -- accounting for 39% of global energy transition investment in 2024.

**Clean energy momentum:** By mid-2025, total renewable capacity reached 2,159 GW (~60% of total power generation capacity). Wind and solar generation rose 27% YoY in H1 2025, enough to cut fossil fuel generation by 2%. Clean energy contributed over 10% of GDP for the first time in 2024.[^2]

**Fossil fuel persistence:** China's 15th Five-Year Plan (2026-2030) softened earlier commitments, replacing pledges to "gradually reduce" coal consumption with language about "promoting the peaking of coal and oil consumption." The plan expands coal-to-chemicals operations and maintains coal as a "strategic reserve" energy source. Fossil fuel SOEs like PetroChina and Shenhua continue to generate enormous free cash flows and pay high dividends, even as the country builds more solar in a year than most nations have in total.[^3]

**Investment implication:** The tension creates a barbell dynamic. Fossil fuel SOEs trade at low P/E with high dividend yields (value play), while clean energy firms like CATL and LONGi offer growth exposure but face fierce domestic competition and margin pressure.

## Key Themes Across All Three Sectors

1. **SOE dominance with valuation discounts:** State ownership is pervasive across all three sectors. SOEs trade at persistent discounts due to governance concerns, policy-directed capital allocation, and geopolitical risk. The "China discount" is most extreme in banks (0.6x P/B) but affects energy and industrial SOEs as well.

2. **Dual listings (A-shares + H-shares):** Most major Chinese companies maintain dual listings on the Shanghai/Shenzhen exchanges (A-shares, accessible to domestic investors) and Hong Kong (H-shares, accessible to international investors). H-shares typically trade at 10-30% discounts to A-shares due to different investor bases and risk perceptions.

3. **Global scale, domestic orientation:** Chinese banks are the world's largest by assets; PetroChina and Sinopec are among the top five oil companies; CRRC is the world's largest train manufacturer; CSCEC is the world's largest construction firm. Yet most revenues are domestic, making them bets on China's economy rather than global diversification plays.

4. **Clean energy transition creates winners and losers:** CATL's $275B market cap reflects the market's confidence in China's EV/battery leadership. Meanwhile, solar (LONGi) faces brutal oversupply. Fossil fuel SOEs remain cash-flow machines but face long-term structural decline. The 15th Five-Year Plan tries to have it both ways -- boosting renewables while maintaining fossil fuel optionality.

5. **Dividend yield as the new bull case:** With growth slowing, many institutional investors are reframing large-cap Chinese SOEs as dividend plays. Banks yielding 5-7%, Shenhua yielding 5%+, and PetroChina increasing payouts have attracted domestic capital seeking yield in a low-rate environment.

6. **Defense sector opacity:** AVIC and NORINCO subsidiaries are publicly listed, but the defense sector remains opaque. Revenue breakdowns between military and civilian segments are limited. The parent companies (unlisted) are far larger than their listed subsidiaries.

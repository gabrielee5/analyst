# Sector Landscape and Weight Evolution of Major Chinese Indices

## Summary

China's major equity indices have undergone a structural transformation over the past 10-15 years, shifting from dominance by state-owned financials and heavy industry ("old China") toward technology, new energy, and consumer sectors ("new China"). The CSI 300 financials weight has roughly halved from ~40% in 2010 to ~22-23% today, while information technology has tripled from ~8% to ~20-23%. The Hang Seng Index mirrors this shift with technology/internet giants like Tencent and Alibaba now dominating what was once a banks-and-property index. Real estate has collapsed from a meaningful index weight to under 1% of the CSI 300 following the Evergrande-triggered property crisis. Despite these shifts, China remains significantly underweight technology relative to the S&P 500 (where IT alone is 31%), and overweight financials and industrials.

---

## Key Findings

1. **Financials dominance has halved**: CSI 300 financials weight fell from ~40%+ (c. 2010) to ~22.6% (Sept 2025). Still the single largest sector, but no longer overwhelming.

2. **Technology tripled in a decade**: IT sector in MSCI China A Onshore went from ~8% (2015) to ~25% (2025). In the CSI 300, IT is now ~19.9%, up from low single digits a decade ago.

3. **Real estate collapsed**: CSI 300 real estate weight shrank to just 0.85% (Sept 2025), down from several percent pre-2021, driven by the Evergrande default and systemic property crisis.

4. **"New energy" emerged as a theme**: CATL (batteries) is now the 3rd-largest CSI 300 constituent at ~2.4% weight. Solar and EV names are distributed across industrials and materials sectors rather than forming a standalone GICS sector.

5. **Consumer staples anchored by Moutai**: Kweichow Moutai remains the single largest CSI 300 stock at ~6.0% weight, larger than any single bank. Consumer staples overall ~9%.

6. **China vs US composition gap**: S&P 500 IT weight is 31% vs CSI 300 at ~20%. China is overweight industrials (18% vs 9%) and materials (9% vs 2%). The MSCI China (offshore-inclusive) has higher consumer discretionary/communication services weight due to Alibaba, Tencent, PDD.

7. **Valuations remain cheap**: CSI 300 overall P/E ~14-15x TTM (March 2026), well below S&P 500 at ~22-24x. China internet trades at roughly half the earnings multiple of US tech peers.

- Financials: ~29% (largest sector)
- Consumer Discretionary: 2nd largest (driven by Alibaba, Meituan, JD.com)
- Information Technology: significant and rising (Tencent, Xiaomi, Lenovo)

In 2025, the HSI rose ~30%. Sector contribution to the rally: financials contributed 40% of index point gains, IT contributed 22%, and non-essential consumer goods contributed 16%.

## Historical Sector Weight Evolution (CSI 300 / China A-Shares)

### Financials: From Dominance to Decline

| Period | Approx. Financials Weight | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 2008-2012 | ~38-42% | Banks and insurers dominated; "Big Four" banks among largest global companies |
| 2015 | ~33-35% | Still dominant; market crash in mid-2015 hit financials hard |
| 2018-2019 | ~28-30% | Gradual dilution as tech and consumer names entered index |
| 2020 | ~25-27% | Pandemic period; tech rally compressed financials' relative weight |
| 2022 | ~23-25% | Property crisis dragged related financial names; tech continued rising |
| 2025 | ~22.6% | Current level; roughly halved from peak |

The financial sector's decline reflects both the relative growth of tech/consumer sectors and the structural headwinds facing Chinese banks (margin compression, property exposure, slower credit growth).

### Information Technology: Triple in a Decade

| Period | Approx. IT Weight | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 2010-2012 | ~5-7% | Minimal tech representation in A-shares |
| 2015 | ~8% | MSCI China A Onshore IT weight confirmed at ~8% |
| 2018-2019 | ~12-14% | STAR Board launched (2019), attracting tech IPOs |
| 2020-2021 | ~15-17% | Tech boom; semiconductor, software names surged |
| 2023 | ~18-20% | AI theme boosted tech names |
| 2025 | ~19.9-23.3% | Varies by index (CSI 300: 19.9%, MSCI China A: 23.3%) |

Key driver: The launch of the STAR Board (Shanghai Science and Technology Innovation Board) in July 2019 channeled tech and biotech IPOs into the A-share market, mechanically increasing IT representation. The ChiNext board in Shenzhen similarly grew tech weight.

### Consumer Staples: The Moutai Effect

Consumer staples weight grew from ~5% (2010) to ~9% (2025), driven largely by Kweichow Moutai's extraordinary price appreciation. Moutai alone is ~6% of the CSI 300, more than many entire sectors. The "baijiu premium" extended to other spirits and food names.

### Healthcare: Steady Growth

Healthcare grew from ~2-3% (2010) to ~6.4% (2025), propelled by aging demographics, biotech innovation, and CDE drug approval reforms post-2017. The weight peaked higher (~8-9%) in 2020-2021 during the pandemic biotech boom before contracting on VBP (volume-based procurement) policy headwinds.

### Real Estate: Collapse

| Period | Approx. Real Estate Weight | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 2018-2019 | ~4-5% | Major developers like Vanke, Poly were significant constituents |
| 2020 | ~3-4% | Still meaningful pre-crisis |
| 2021 | ~2-3% | Evergrande default (Dec 2021); sector stress begins |
| 2022-2023 | ~1-2% | Country Garden, Kaisa, Sunac defaults; sector-wide collapse |
| 2025 | ~0.85% | Near-negligible; housing starts down >60% from pre-pandemic levels |

The property crisis, still ongoing in its fourth year as of 2025, wiped out what was once a meaningful index sector. Real estate was ~25% of GDP as recently as 2024, but its equity market representation has been decimated.

### New Energy / Green Sector

"New energy" is not a standalone GICS sector but is distributed across:
- **Industrials**: CATL (world's largest battery maker, ~2.4% of CSI 300)
- **Materials**: Lithium producers (Ganfeng Lithium, Tianqi Lithium)
- **Consumer Discretionary**: BYD (EVs)
- **Utilities**: Some renewable energy generators

China's dominance in new energy is extraordinary:
- >60% of global EV sales
- >90% of global solar production chain
- >50% of global industrial robot installations
- Clean energy investment >$625 billion in 2024, nearly doubled since 2015

The CSI New Energy Index (a thematic sub-index) surged in 2020-2021 then corrected sharply in 2022-2024 as overcapacity concerns emerged.

| Financials | 22.6 | 12.9 | +9.7 |

| Consumer Discretionary | 7.6 | 10.4 | -2.8 |
| Communication Services | 1.0 | 11.0 | -10.0 |

**Key structural differences:**
- China is massively underweight communication services because Tencent, Alibaba, PDD, Meituan (the Chinese "big tech") are listed in Hong Kong, not A-shares. The MSCI China (offshore-inclusive) has comm services at 22% and consumer discretionary at 28%.
- China is overweight industrials and materials, reflecting the manufacturing/infrastructure-heavy economy.
- China financials are nearly double the S&P 500 weight, but have been converging.
- The "tech gap" narrows substantially when using MSCI China (which includes HK/ADR tech) instead of CSI 300.

## The "Old China" to "New China" Structural Shift

**"Old China" sectors** (banks, insurance, property, heavy industry, resources):
- Combined weight in CSI 300: ~45-50% (2010) --> ~35% (2025)
- Still dominant in absolute terms but declining relatively
- Characterized by SOE ownership, low valuations, high dividends

**"New China" sectors** (technology, new energy/EVs, biotech, advanced manufacturing):
- Combined weight in CSI 300: ~15-20% (2010) --> ~35-40% (2025)
- Driven by STAR Board/ChiNext IPOs, CATL/BYD growth, semiconductor self-sufficiency drive
- Higher valuations, lower dividends, private enterprise-heavy

**Catalysts for the shift:**
1. STAR Board launch (2019) -- tech IPO channel
2. "Dual circulation" policy -- prioritizing domestic tech capability
3. US-China tech war -- semiconductor self-sufficiency imperative
4. Carbon neutrality pledge (2060 target) -- new energy investment boom
5. Property crisis (2021-present) -- destroyed old economy weight
6. "Common prosperity" campaign -- regulatory pressure on some old economy sectors

# Research Brief: Bethlehem Steel Corporation

## Company Name & Identifiers
- **Company**: Bethlehem Steel Corporation
- **Ticker**: BS (NYSE) — delisted 2001
- **SIC Code**: 3312 (Steel Works, Blast Furnaces)
- **Headquarters**: Bethlehem, Pennsylvania
- **Incorporated**: 1904 (predecessor entities from 1857)

## Analysis Scope
Full corporate lifecycle analysis spanning 1857 to 2003 — from the founding of the Saucona Iron Company through Bethlehem Steel's liquidation. This is a 146-year arc covering America's industrialization, two world wars, post-war prosperity, deindustrialization, and bankruptcy.

## Core Analytical Question
How did America's second-largest steel producer — a company that built the Golden Gate Bridge, armed two world wars, and employed 300,000 workers at its peak — go from industrial titan to Chapter 11 liquidation? What were the financial, strategic, competitive, and structural forces at each inflection point, and what lessons does this trajectory hold for understanding industrial economics?

## Key Metrics to Track
- Revenue and net income across major eras
- Steel production volume (tons shipped per year)
- Employment levels (peak and trough)
- Stock price performance across decades
- Debt-to-equity and leverage ratios (later periods)
- Pension and retiree healthcare obligations (OPEB liabilities)
- Capital expenditure and capacity utilization
- Market share vs. domestic and international competitors

## Peer Group

- **National Steel Corporation** — integrated competitor

- **Inland Steel** — midwest integrated producer
- **Japanese steelmakers** (Nippon Steel, JFE) — import competition
- **Korean steelmakers** (POSCO) — late-period import competition

## Time Periods of Interest
1. **Founding Era (1857–1904)** — Iron to steel transition, early growth
2. **Schwab Era (1904–1920)** — Charles M. Schwab's acquisition and transformation
3. **World War I (1914–1918)** — Military contracts and wartime production boom
4. **Interwar Period (1920–1939)** — Expansion, Depression, labor battles
5. **World War II (1939–1945)** — Peak wartime production, shipbuilding
6. **Post-War Boom (1945–1970)** — Infrastructure spending, highway system, skyscrapers
7. **Crisis Period (1970–1990)** — Import competition, mini-mills, steel crisis of 1982
8. **Terminal Decline (1990–2003)** — Restructuring attempts, bankruptcy filing, liquidation

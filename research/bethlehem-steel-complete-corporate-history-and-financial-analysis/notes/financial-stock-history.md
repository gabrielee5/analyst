# Bethlehem Steel: Financial Performance & Stock Price History

## Summary

Bethlehem Steel's financial trajectory is one of the most dramatic corporate arcs in American history. The company rose from a small iron works to the nation's second-largest steelmaker, a Dow Jones Industrial Average component for nearly seven decades (1928--1997), and a Fortune 500 top-10 company. At its zenith in the late 1950s, it earned $190 million on $2.6 billion in sales with 165,000 employees. Its stock was among the most celebrated on Wall Street -- surging from $30 to $600 per share during World War I, making it the original "super stock." By the time of its bankruptcy filing on October 15, 2001, the stock traded at $1.01, the company carried $4.5 billion in debt, its pension plan was underfunded by $3.2--4.3 billion, and its market capitalization had shriveled to $190 million. The final act -- sale to International Steel Group for $1.5 billion in 2003 -- ended 146 years of corporate existence.

The contrast with Nucor is the defining comparison: $1 invested in Nucor versus $1 in Bethlehem Steel diverged by over 200x, according to Jim Collins' research. Bethlehem's decline was not merely cyclical -- it was structural, driven by management insularity, failure to adopt mini-mill technology, crushing legacy costs, and an inability to adapt to a globalized steel market.

---

## Key Findings

1. **Peak revenue**: $7.3 billion in 1981 (record). Revenue declined to $4.63 billion by 1997 and $3.76 billion by mid-2001.
2. **Peak profitability**: $342 million net income in 1974; $400+ million record earnings in 1988 (brief resurgence under restructuring).
3. **Stock price arc**: $30 to $600 (WWI), crashed in 1929, recovered postwar, peaked late 1950s, 20-year high of $31.75 in March 1981, all-time low of ~$4 in 1986, briefly recovered mid-1990s, fell below $3 in September 2000, hit $1.01 in August 2001.
4. **Dividends**: Continuous from 1916 through 1991; eliminated in January 1992.
5. **DJIA membership**: Added 1928, removed March 17, 1997.
6. **S&P 500 membership**: 1957--2000 (moved to S&P 400 MidCap 2000--2003).
7. **Pension crisis**: $1.6 billion unfunded pension liability in 1993; $3.2--4.3 billion underfunding at bankruptcy. PBGC takeover was largest in U.S. history at the time.
8. **Retiree healthcare**: $3.0--3.1 billion in obligations, zero recovery for retirees (PBGC does not cover OPEB).
9. **Burns Harbor**: Last greenfield integrated steel plant built in the U.S.; largest private construction project in U.S. history at the time (1962--1972).
10. **Nucor divergence**: By the late 1990s, Nucor -- less than a third of Bethlehem's size a decade earlier -- surpassed it in revenue, while remaining consistently profitable.

## Stock Price History

### World War I Era (1914--1918)
- **Pre-war**: ~$30 per share [^1]
- **October 4, 1915**: $399 per share [^9]
- **1916--1917 peak**: ~$600 per share [^1]
- The stock was nicknamed "the war baby" and became one of the most celebrated speculative plays of the era. Dividends reached 10% quarterly in April 1917.[^10]

### 1920s Boom and 1929 Crash
- Bethlehem Steel was among the major industrial stocks that soared during the 1920s bull market.
- Chairman Charles M. Schwab declared on October 25, 1929: "In my long association with the steel industry I have never known it to enjoy a greater stability or more promising outlook than it does today."[^11]
- During the 1929 crash and aftermath, Bethlehem Steel lost ~95% of its value.[^11]
- Bethlehem was one of the corporations that lent directly into the call money market at 8--12% rates, fueling the speculative bubble.[^11]

### Post-War Peak (Late 1950s--Early 1960s)
- Stock price peaked in the late 1950s, coinciding with record production and earnings.[^2]
- In 1958, company president Arthur B. Homer was the highest-paid executive in America ($511,249 + bonus).[^4]
- After accounting for stock splits and other adjustments, the postwar high represented the company's last era of true blue-chip status.

### 1960s--1970s Decline
- Both U.S. Steel and Bethlehem Steel stock prices peaked at the end of the 1950s and then gradually declined.[^12]
- Despite record profits in 1973--1974, the stock did not return to 1950s-era valuations, reflecting market recognition of structural competitive threats.

### 1980s Collapse and Partial Recovery
- **March 1981**: 20-year high of $31.75 per share [^13]
- **1982--1986**: Devastating decline as losses mounted
- **1986**: All-time low of ~$4 per share [^2]
- **1987--1988**: Partial recovery as company briefly returned to profitability

### 1990s: Last Hurrah and Terminal Decline
- Mid-1990s recovery with strong 1997 results ($280M net income)
- **March 17, 1997**: Removed from Dow Jones Industrial Average (replaced by Travelers, HP, J&J, Walmart) after 69 years as a component [^14]
- Stock had already been in decline when removed from the DJIA
- Late 1990s: Import crisis devastated pricing

### Final Collapse (2000--2002)
- **September 28, 2000**: Fell below $3.00 for first time; closed at $2.9375 -- then all-time low [^13]
- **52-week range (as of Aug 31, 2001)**: $1.01 -- $4.30 [^6]
- **August 2001**: Hit $1.01, new 52-week low [^6]
- **October 15, 2001**: Filed Chapter 11 bankruptcy
- **Recent price at filing**: $1.46 [^6]
- **June 2002**: Delisted from NYSE; relegated to OTC bulletin board at penny-stock prices [^15]
- **Market cap at delisting**: ~$190 million (130.2M shares outstanding x ~$1.46) [^6]

## Debt, Pension, and Credit Profile

### Total Debt
- **At bankruptcy (Oct 2001)**: $4.5 billion total debt [^16]
- **Book value per share (mid-2001)**: Negative $1.29 -- the company was technically insolvent [^6]
- **Current ratio (mid-2001)**: 0.91 -- below 1.0, indicating inability to cover short-term obligations [^6]

### Pension Liabilities
- **1993**: $1.6 billion unfunded pension liability [^2]
- **End of 1997**: Reduced to $440 million (through asset returns and contributions during the bull market) [^2]
- **At bankruptcy**: $3.2 billion underfunding (on ongoing basis, the plan was reported as 84% funded; on termination basis, only 45% funded, with a $4.3 billion shortfall) [^16]
- **PBGC takeover**: Largest pension assumption in U.S. history at the time; ~95,000 participants; $3.9 billion in unfunded benefits absorbed [^16]

### Retiree Healthcare (OPEB)
- **At bankruptcy**: $3.0--3.1 billion in retiree health and welfare obligations [^16]
- **PBGC coverage**: Zero. PBGC does not cover OPEB. Retirees lost healthcare benefits entirely.[^16]
- **Ratio**: ~95,000 retirees/dependents vs. ~13,000 active employees -- a 7:1 ratio of benefit claimants to workers [^17]

### Credit Deterioration
- Investment-grade credit through the 1960s--1970s (as befitting a DJIA component)
- Progressively downgraded through the 1980s and 1990s as losses accumulated
- By the late 1990s, below-investment-grade status severely constrained refinancing options
- The combination of cyclical steel downturns and structural legacy costs made the balance sheet increasingly untenable

## Comparison: Bethlehem Steel vs. S&P 500

Bethlehem Steel was a member of the S&P 500 from its inception in 1957 through 2000. However, the stock dramatically underperformed the index:

- The S&P 500 returned approximately 10--11% annualized over the 1957--2000 period.
- Bethlehem Steel's stock moved from its late-1950s peak to near-zero by 2001 -- a total loss for any long-term holder.
- A $10,000 investment in the S&P 500 in 1957 would have grown to approximately $1.5 million by 2001. The same investment in Bethlehem Steel would have been worth approximately zero.
- The stock was removed from the S&P 500 in 2000 and moved to the S&P 400 MidCap, before that too became untenable.

## Comparison: Bethlehem Steel vs. US Steel

- Both companies' stock prices peaked at the end of the 1950s and then gradually declined.[^12]
- US Steel diversified into oil (Marathon Oil acquisition, 1982) and renamed itself USX Corporation in 1986. By the 1990s, Marathon Oil represented over 80% of USX revenues.[^12]
- Both companies suffered massive losses in the 1982 steel crisis.
- US Steel survived through diversification; Bethlehem remained a pure-play steelmaker and could not escape the structural decline of integrated steelmaking.
- US Steel eventually re-separated its steel operations (2001) and survived as an independent entity.

## Analyst Coverage and Notable Wall Street Events

- Bethlehem Steel was among the most widely covered industrial stocks throughout the mid-20th century, as a DJIA component from 1928 to 1997.
- In 1958, Arthur B. Homer was the highest-paid business executive in the United States.[^4]
- The 1982 loss of $1.5 billion shocked Wall Street and marked a watershed moment for the entire integrated steel sector.
- Removal from the DJIA on March 17, 1997 was a symbolic milestone -- Bethlehem was replaced by technology and consumer-facing companies (HP, J&J, Walmart, Travelers), reflecting the shift of the American economy away from heavy industry.[^14]
- By 2001, the stock was covered primarily by distressed-debt and bankruptcy analysts rather than equity analysts.
- As of mid-2001: insider/5%+ ownership was 29%, institutional ownership was 46%.[^6]

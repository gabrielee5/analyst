# Bethlehem Steel: The Post-War Golden Age (1945--1970)

## Summary

The quarter-century following World War II represented Bethlehem Steel's apex as an industrial enterprise. Emerging from the war with over 300,000 employees and $1.33 billion in annual sales, the company rode a massive wave of postwar reconstruction, consumer demand, and infrastructure spending --- most notably the Interstate Highway System and the urban building boom --- to become a cornerstone of American industrial might. Peak postwar production came in 1957, with 19+ million tons of raw steel and $2.6 billion in revenue. The company ranked No. 8 on the inaugural Fortune 500 (1955) and its president was the highest-paid executive in America (1958). Bethlehem was the structural steel behind some of the most iconic infrastructure in U.S. history: the Golden Gate Bridge, the George Washington Bridge, Rockefeller Center, the Waldorf Astoria, the Verrazzano-Narrows Bridge, Madison Square Garden, and Hoover Dam.

Yet the seeds of decline were unmistakable by 1970. The 1959 steel strike opened the door to imports that never receded. Management clung to open-hearth technology, abandoned a nearly-complete continuous casting project (wasting ~$10 million), and prioritized a $35 million vanity headquarters over modernization. The loss of the World Trade Center steel contract in 1967 was a symbolic turning point. By decade's end, the company was spending heavily on capacity expansion (Burns Harbor, the $275 million "Big L" blast furnace) while failing to invest in the process technologies that Japanese and German competitors had already mastered. The postwar golden age was, in retrospect, a period of peak output masking strategic rot.

## Key Findings

### 1. Infrastructure Boom and Role in National Building

Bethlehem Steel was the dominant supplier of structural steel for America's postwar construction boom. The company helped build the Interstate Highway System (authorized 1956), supplying structural steel for bridges, overpasses, and highway infrastructure throughout the 1950s and 1960s. [Source: [Bethlehem Steel - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bethlehem_Steel)]

Bethlehem had pioneered commercially successful wide-flange structural shapes beginning in 1908, which became the standard for skyscraper and bridge construction. This gave the company a dominant position in structural steel that persisted through the postwar era. [Source: [Lehigh Valley News](https://www.lehighvalleynews.com/bethlehem/lehigh-valley-250-silent-now-bethlehem-steels-roar-still-echoes-across-time)]

A signature project of this era: the Chase Manhattan Bank Building (now 28 Liberty Street) in lower Manhattan, for which Bethlehem fabricated and erected 53,000 tons of steelwork --- a massive single-project contract completed c. 1961. [Source: [Internet Archive - 60 Stories of Steel](https://archive.org/details/BethlehemSteelCompnayCCA80372)]

### 2. Peak Production Volumes

- **1957 (peak postwar year):** 19+ million tons of raw steel produced. [Source: [Company Histories](https://www.company-histories.com/Bethlehem-Steel-Corporation-Company-History.html)]
- **1964:** Record output of 19,436,000 tons of raw steel. [Source: [Alicia Patterson Foundation](https://aliciapatterson.org/john-strohmeyer/the-demise-of-big-steel/)]
- **1966:** 21,275,000 net tons of raw steel produced; 13,849,000 net tons shipped. [Source: [Bethlehem Steel 1967 Annual Report via Internet Archive](https://archive.org/stream/bethlehemsteelcompanyannualreports/bethlehemsteel1967_djvu.txt)]
- **1967:** 20,525,000 net tons of raw steel produced; 13,056,000 net tons shipped. [Source: ibid.]
- **1973 (all-time record):** 23.7 million tons of raw steel produced; 16.3 million tons of finished steel shipped. [Source: [Bethlehem Steel - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bethlehem_Steel)]
- **Rated annual capacity by late 1950s:** ~23 million tons. [Source: [Company Histories](https://www.company-histories.com/Bethlehem-Steel-Corporation-Company-History.html)]

### 3. Peak Employment

- **1945 (wartime peak):** Over 300,000 employees company-wide, including up to 180,000 in shipbuilding alone. [Source: [Bethlehem Steel - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bethlehem_Steel)]
- **Late 1950s (peacetime peak):** ~165,000 full-time employees company-wide. [Source: [Company Histories](https://www.company-histories.com/Bethlehem-Steel-Corporation-Company-History.html)]
- **1957:** Sparrows Point alone employed ~30,000 (plus ~4,000 at the adjacent shipyard), making it the largest steel plant in the world. [Source: [Sparrows Point - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sparrows_Point,_Maryland)]
- **1965:** Lackawanna plant employed ~20,000 workers. [Source: [Bethlehem Steel in Lackawanna](https://buffaloah.com/h/beth/)]
- **1966--1967:** Monthly average employees company-wide were 133,000 (1966) and 131,000 (1967). [Source: [1967 Annual Report](https://archive.org/stream/bethlehemsteelcompanyannualreports/bethlehemsteel1967_djvu.txt)]

### 5. Market Share

Bethlehem was consistently the second-largest U.S. steel producer, behind only U.S. Steel. U.S. Steel averaged ~30% of American steel output in the mid-1950s, with Bethlehem holding an estimated 15--16% share (based on ~19M tons produced in a ~120M-ton domestic market). Together, the top two producers controlled nearly half the market. The remainder was split among Republic Steel, Jones & Laughlin, National Steel, Armco, Inland Steel, and Youngstown Sheet & Tube. [Source: [Construction Physics - US Steel History](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/no-inventions-no-innovations-a-history); [Bethlehem Steel - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bethlehem_Steel)]

### 6. Capital Investment Decisions

**What was invested in:**
- **Burns Harbor, IN (1962--1972):** The last fully integrated greenfield steel plant ever built in the United States. Phase 1 (1962--1964) included the world's first 160-inch plate mill. Cold-rolled sheet, tin mill, and 80-inch hot mill followed within two more years. First blast furnace lit in late 1969, second in early 1972, providing ~10,000 tons/day iron-making capacity. This was the largest private construction project in U.S. history at the time. [Source: [Indiana Historical Bureau](https://www.in.gov/history/state-historical-markers/find-a-marker/find-historical-markers-by-county/indiana-historical-markers-by-county/legacy-of-steel-burns-harbor-steel-plant/); [Burns Harbor Local History](https://www.burnsharbor-in.gov/203/Local-History)]
- **$3 billion modernization and expansion program (1960s):** Launched by Arthur B. Homer after Eugene Grace's death in 1960. Focused primarily on expanding tonnage capacity. [Source: [FundingUniverse](https://www.fundinguniverse.com/company-histories/bethlehem-steel-corporation-history/)]
- **"Big L" blast furnace at Sparrows Point:** Cost $275 million (early 1970s). [Source: [FundingUniverse](https://www.fundinguniverse.com/company-histories/bethlehem-steel-corporation-history/)]
- **Martin Tower headquarters (1969--1972):** 21-story cruciform-shaped office building in Bethlehem, PA. Cost ~$35 million. Designed so that "innumerable vice presidents" could have corner offices. [Source: [Alicia Patterson Foundation](https://aliciapatterson.org/john-strohmeyer/the-demise-of-big-steel/); [Martin Tower - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Tower)]
- **BOF adoption (1964):** Bethlehem installed its first basic oxygen furnaces at the Lackawanna plant in 1964 --- the same year U.S. Steel adopted the technology. This was roughly a decade after Austrian steelmakers commercialized the process (1952) and well behind Japan's adoption. [Source: [Basic Oxygen Steelmaking - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_oxygen_steelmaking)]

**What was NOT invested in (critical failures):**
- **Continuous casting:** Bethlehem abandoned continuous casting experiments twice in the 1960s. A four-strand billet caster project in Johnstown, 90% complete, was scrapped because the plant was "physically ill-suited" --- exactly as the research committee had warned (they had recommended Steelton or Seattle instead). Approximately $10 million was wasted. The company never fully adopted continuous casting. [Source: [Alicia Patterson Foundation](https://aliciapatterson.org/john-strohmeyer/the-demise-of-big-steel/)]
- **Process innovation broadly:** Management "did not engage in product research, innovation, or reorganization," instead relying on "continual price increases to protect profits." [Source: [Encyclopedia.com](https://www.encyclopedia.com/social-sciences-and-law/economics-business-and-labor/businesses-and-occupations/bethlehem-steel-corp)]

### 7. Plant Operations

| Plant | Location | Key Facts (Postwar Era) |
|-------|----------|------------------------|
| **Bethlehem Works** | Bethlehem, PA | Headquarters and original plant. Structural steel fabrication hub. Home of wide-flange beam production. Last blast furnace shut down Nov. 18, 1995. |
| **Sparrows Point** | Baltimore, MD | Largest steel plant in the world by 1957. 10 blast furnaces, rated capacity 8.2M short tons/year of ingot steel. ~30,000 employees at peak (1950s). Adjacent shipyard employed additional thousands. Five square miles of industrial operations. |
| **Lackawanna Works** | Lackawanna, NY | ~20,000 employees (1965). Site of first BOF installation (1964). Rolled uranium fuel rods for federal government (1949--1952). Began major cuts in 1977; most operations closed by 1983. |
| **Burns Harbor** | Burns Harbor, IN | Built 1962--1972 as greenfield integrated plant. World's first 160-inch plate mill. Became Bethlehem's largest plant. Last integrated greenfield steel plant built in the U.S. |
| **Johnstown Works** | Johnstown, PA | Acquired 1923 (from Cambria Iron Co., founded 1852). Major producer of railroad freight cars, rails, and structural steel. Site of the abandoned continuous casting experiment. Closed 1992; management buyout created Johnstown America Industries (1991). |

Additional facilities: Steelton, PA; Williamsport, PA; Lebanon, PA; South San Francisco, CA; Los Angeles, CA; Seattle, WA. Shipyards at Quincy (MA), San Francisco, San Pedro (CA), Staten Island (NY), Baltimore, and Beaumont (TX). [Source: [Bethlehem Steel - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bethlehem_Steel)]

### 8. Stock Price and Market Performance

- Bethlehem Steel traded on the NYSE under ticker symbol "BS" from 1906 until delisting in 2002.
- The stock was a component of the Dow Jones Industrial Average for approximately seven decades, added circa 1928, removed on March 17, 1997 (replaced by Travelers Inc.). [Source: [Dogs of the Dow](https://www.dogsofthedow.com/djdelete.htm); [Slate](https://slate.com/business/2002/06/bethlehem-steel-from-a-list-to-delist.html)]
- During World War I, the stock famously rose from $30 to $600 (pre-split), one of history's great stock runs. [Source: [AMP Forum](https://forum.ampfutures.com/AMPforum/trading-jokes-events-chitchat/jokes-trade-shows-chit-chat/8335-trading-history-bethlehem-steel-one-of-histories-super-stocks-10-4-1915-399)]
- Specific postwar stock prices are not widely available in secondary sources, but the stock's trajectory tracked the company's peak in the late 1950s and gradual decline through the 1960s. The DJIA removal in 1997 and bankruptcy filing in 2001 marked the final chapter.

### 9. Seeds of Decline Visible by 1970

1. **The 1959 Steel Strike:** 500,000 workers walked out for 116 days (July 15 -- Nov. 7, 1959), shutting down 85% of U.S. steel production. Steel imports jumped from 2 million tons (1958) to 5 million tons (1959) and never returned to pre-strike levels. U.S. industrial buyers discovered Japanese and Korean steel was cheaper even after shipping costs. This was the pivotal event that opened the import floodgate. [Source: [Steel Strike of 1959](https://nbrfof.org/timeline/steel-strike-of-1959/); [Steel strike of 1959 - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel_strike_of_1959)]

2. **1960: First year U.S. imported more steel than it exported.** A structural shift, not a cyclical blip. [Source: [Encyclopedia.com](https://www.encyclopedia.com/social-sciences-and-law/economics-business-and-labor/businesses-and-occupations/bethlehem-steel-corp)]

3. **Technology gap:** Japan and Germany rebuilt their steel industries with basic oxygen furnaces and continuous casting from the start. Bethlehem and other U.S. integrated mills were still running open-hearth furnaces that were slower, costlier, and produced inferior steel. Bethlehem's belated BOF adoption (1964) came a decade late; its continuous casting abandonment was worse. [Source: [Alicia Patterson Foundation](https://aliciapatterson.org/john-strohmeyer/the-demise-of-big-steel/)]

4. **Management complacency:** Leadership relied on price increases rather than productivity improvements. Homer's $3 billion expansion program focused on more tonnage capacity, not better process technology. The $35 million Martin Tower headquarters (1969--1972) epitomized misplaced priorities. [Source: [Encyclopedia.com](https://www.encyclopedia.com/social-sciences-and-law/economics-business-and-labor/businesses-and-occupations/bethlehem-steel-corp)]

5. **Labor cost escalation:** Generous union contracts from the postwar era (supplemental unemployment benefits, 13-week sabbatical vacations for senior employees every five years, full health coverage) made U.S. steel increasingly uncompetitive. By the early 1980s, the average steelworker earned $26.29/hour including benefits. [Source: [Alicia Patterson Foundation](https://aliciapatterson.org/john-strohmeyer/the-demise-of-big-steel/)]

6. **Pension underfunding:** Management "failed to adequately invest in the company's pension plans during the 1950s," creating a growing ratio of retirees to active workers that would become a crushing liability. [Source: [Bethlehem Steel - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bethlehem_Steel)]

7. **1967 World Trade Center contract loss:** Bethlehem lost the bid to supply steel for the original World Trade Center. The Port Authority split the contract among multiple smaller suppliers to save costs --- a humiliation for a company that had built the skyline. Contracts (one alone worth 50,000 tons) went to competitors in Seattle, St. Louis, New York, and Illinois. [Source: [Bethlehem Steel - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bethlehem_Steel)]

8. **Rising environmental compliance costs:** The environmental movement of the late 1960s imposed new costs on the company's aging, polluting facilities --- costs that newer foreign mills did not face. [Source: [Encyclopedia.com](https://www.encyclopedia.com/social-sciences-and-law/economics-business-and-labor/businesses-and-occupations/bethlehem-steel-corp)]

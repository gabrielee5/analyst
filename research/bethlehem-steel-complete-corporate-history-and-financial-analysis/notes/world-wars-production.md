# Bethlehem Steel: World War I & World War II Production and Financial Impact

## Summary

Bethlehem Steel was the single most important private military-industrial enterprise in America across both World Wars. During WWI, the company was the nation's No. 1 munitions maker, producing over 60% of Allied finished artillery and guns, with stock rising from ~$30 to $600 and Allied orders totaling $500 million. During WWII, Bethlehem became the nation's largest defense contractor: its 15 shipyards built 1,121 ships (nearly one-fifth of the U.S. Navy's two-ocean fleet), produced 73.4 million tons of steel, and employed 283,765 people at peak. Sales surged from a pre-war $135 million to $1.33 billion by 1945. The company emerged from both wars as the second-largest steelmaker in America (behind only U.S. Steel) but with a philosophy of relentless capacity expansion that sowed seeds of long-term overcapacity.

---

## Key Findings: World War I (1914-1918)

### Military Production

- Bethlehem Steel was **the No. 1 munitions maker in America** during WWI and had been "frenetically serving the Allied cause since the war began in 1914." [^1]
- The company produced **over 60% of Allied finished artillery and guns**, making it the primary producer of munitions and armaments for the Allies. [^2]
- Products manufactured included: guns, shells, armor plate, submarine parts, naval guns, and other ordnance. [^1]
- Bethlehem filled orders for **guns and munitions, armor, and ordnance placed by the British, French, and Russian governments**. [^3]
- When the Lusitania was torpedoed in May 1915, it was carrying **1,250 cases of Bethlehem-manufactured artillery shells** headed for the British army. [^1]
- The company had been producing military hardware since the 1890s, with contracts for breech-loading rifled naval guns dating to 1892. [^4]

### Financial Impact

- **Stock price**: Rose from approximately **$30 to $600** during the war period. On October 4, 1915, the stock traded at **$399**. By 1916 it reached a peak of **$700**. [^5][^6]
- **Allied orders**: Bethlehem filled orders **totaling $500 million** throughout the conflict. A single British order of **$135 million** was the largest in company history to that point. [^7]
- **Annual profits**: In 1910, Bethlehem made approximately **$2 million/year** in profit. From 1915-1929, the company typically earned about **$15 million**, and in peak years as much as **$40 million**. [^7]
- **Profit margins on gross sales**: Reported at **49%, 58%, and 46%** for 1916, 1917, and 1918, respectively. [^8]
- **Dividends**: Common stock dividends reached **7.5% quarterly** on the 150,000 original shares during 1916-1917, touching **10% quarterly** in April 1917. [^9]
- **Wartime acquisitions (1916)**: Bolstered by war profits, Bethlehem acquired American Iron and Steel Manufacturing Company, Pennsylvania Steel Company, and Maryland Steel Company. [^3]

### Workforce Expansion

- At the start of WWI, Bethlehem employed approximately **15,600 people** with annual steelmaking capacity of **1.1 million tons**. [^2]
- By the height of WWI production, **31,000 workers** were employed at the South Bethlehem plant alone, manufacturing guns, shells, armor plate, and submarine parts. [^1]
- This represents roughly a **doubling of workforce** at the main plant during the war.
- Women were employed operating drill presses and in other production roles. [^1]

### Government Contracts and War Profiteering

- **Emergency Fleet Corporation**: Between April and December 1918, Charles M. Schwab served as director general of the Emergency Fleet Corporation at the appointment of President Woodrow Wilson, charged with expediting national shipbuilding. Schwab replaced cost-plus-profit contracts with fixed-price contracts. [^7]
- **U.S. v. Bethlehem Steel Corp. (1942)**: The Supreme Court case concerned 13 wartime shipbuilding contracts from 1917-1918. Bethlehem received total profits of **over $20 million** under these contracts and sought an additional **$7.5 million** (including ~$3.8 million in bonuses). The Court upheld the profits as not unconscionable. [^10]
- **Nye Committee (1934-1936)**: The Senate Special Committee Investigating the Munitions Industry held **93 public hearings** and interrogated over **200 witnesses**. Publications like *Merchants of Death* (1934) labeled Bethlehem Steel and DuPont as "merchants of death." The committee found that arms firms (including Bethlehem) **amassed $1.2 billion in combined profits from 1914-1918**. Eugene G. Grace testified before the committee, arguing against government conscription of private industry in wartime. [^11][^12]
- The Nye Committee found "ample evidence" of enormous profits but "little support for the theory that the industry had conspired to draw the nation into war." [^12]

## Key Findings: World War II (1939-1945)

### Shipbuilding Program

- **Total ships built: 1,121 vessels** across Bethlehem Shipbuilding Corporation's **15 shipyards** -- more than any other builder during WWII. [^13]
  - Note: Some sources cite **1,085 vessels built** (with an additional 37,778 ships repaired, converted, or serviced). The 1,121 figure appears in Wikipedia and other major references; the 1,085 figure appears in the PA Explore History marker for Eugene Grace. The discrepancy may relate to whether certain converted/completed vessels are counted. [^13][^14]
- Ships built represented **nearly one-fifth of the U.S. Navy's two-ocean fleet**. [^13]
- **Types of vessels**: aircraft carriers, battleships, heavy cruisers, light cruisers, destroyers, destroyer escorts, oil tankers, Liberty ships, Victory ships, LSTs (Landing Ship, Tank), and cargo vessels. [^13][^14]
- In **1943**, Bethlehem launched **380 vessels** -- Grace had promised President Roosevelt one ship per day and exceeded that commitment by 15 ships. [^13][^14]

#### Bethlehem-Fairfield Shipyard, Baltimore

- **Operational period**: February 1941 to 1945. [^15]
- **Location**: South shore of the Middle Branch, Patapsco River, Baltimore, MD. Covered **136 acres** with **13 slipways** (later expanded to **16**), serviced by **75 cranes**, **10 locomotives**, and **200+ flatcars**. [^15]
- **Total vessels produced**: **523 ships** -- including **384 Liberty ships**, **94 Victory ships**, and **45 LSTs**. [^15]
- **Employment**: Approximately **27,000 workers**. [^15]
- The **SS Patrick Henry**, launched on **September 27, 1941** ("Liberty Fleet Day"), was the **first Liberty ship built in the United States**. Initial construction time: 244 days. [^15][^16]
- In August-December 1942, Bethlehem temporarily devoted 12 of 16 Fairfield slipways to LST production (45 total). [^15]

#### Bethlehem-Hingham Shipyard, Massachusetts

- Specialized in **destroyer escorts**: built **132 destroyer escorts** before switching to landing ships in 1944. [^17]
- Total production: **277 ships**, including one destroyer escort delivered in just **23 days**. [^17]
- In 1943 alone, **90 destroyer escorts** were built. [^17]
- Employment peaked at over **23,500 workers**. [^17]

#### Fore River Shipyard, Quincy, Massachusetts

- **Surpassed all other American shipyards in tonnage delivered** during WWII. [^18]
- Built: a replacement USS Lexington, **3 Essex-class aircraft carriers**, **7 light cruisers**, **7 Baltimore-class heavy cruisers**, **18 destroyer escorts**, and **46 LSTs**. [^18]

#### Sparrows Point Shipyard, Baltimore

- Built **101 vessels** of **16 different classes** during WWII. [^19]
- Employed up to **8,000 workers** in the shipyard (separate from the steel mill). [^19]

#### Staten Island Yard, New York

- Produced **44 destroyer ships** beginning December 1940, **39 completed during the war**. [^18]
- Expanded with **$6 million** in U.S. Navy funding, gaining capacity for vessels up to **516 feet**. [^20]

### Steel Production

- From 1940 to 1945, Bethlehem produced **73.4 million tons of steel**. [^14]
- Between 1941-1944, Eugene Grace pushed production to **101% of usual capacity**. [^21]
- Bethlehem produced **more steel than England's entire output** during the war. [^14]
- **Steelmaking capacity** had been boosted to **8.5 million tons** annually by the late 1920s; wartime expansion pushed it further. [^21]

### Specialized Military Production

- **70% of airplane cylinder forgings** for the U.S. armed forces. [^13]
- **25% (one-quarter) of all warship armor plate**. [^13]
- **33% (one-third) of all large cannon forgings**. [^13]
- Additional products: bomb casings, armor-piercing shells, airplane parts and engines, airfield landing mats, submarine air flasks, railway equipment, tank forgings, wire ropes and cables. [^4]

### Peak Employment

- **283,765 employees in 1943** across all operations. [^14]
- Of these, approximately **180,000** were in shipbuilding. [^13]
- Over **31,000** at the Bethlehem, PA headquarters plant. [^14]
- Approximately **25,000 women** joined the workforce. [^14]
- **67,000 former employees** served in the U.S. armed forces. [^14]
- Average worker wage: **$1.30/hour** by 1943. [^14]

### Revenue and Profits

- **Pre-war gross sales**: approximately **$135 million**. [^21]
- **1945 gross sales**: **$1.33 billion** -- a nearly **10x increase**. [^21]
- **Wartime production contracts**: Bethlehem ranked **7th among all U.S. corporations** in total contract value. [^13]
- Contracts for non-shipbuilding military supplies totaled nearly **$1.5 billion** (bomb casings, gun forgings, airplane parts, etc.). [^4]
- Eugene Grace, upon learning of Germany's invasion of Poland in 1939, reportedly told associates the company would "make some money." [^14]

### Government-Funded Capacity Expansion

- The U.S. government funded expansion of the Sparrows Point mill due to its strategic East Coast location. [^19]
- The Staten Island yard received **$6 million** from the U.S. Navy for expansion. [^20]
- The Bethlehem-Fairfield and Bethlehem-Hingham shipyards were constructed from scratch as wartime emergency facilities, funded through the Maritime Commission's **Emergency Shipbuilding Program** and the **Long Range Shipbuilding Program**. [^15][^17]
- The **Two Ocean Navy Act** (1940) and **Lend-Lease Act** (1941) drove massive contract expansion. [^20]

## Comparative: Bethlehem Steel vs. U.S. Steel

| Metric | Bethlehem Steel | U.S. Steel |
|--------|----------------|------------|
| **Steel tonnage (WWII)** | 73.4 million tons (1940-1945) | Larger total (exact figure varies by source) |
| **Industry ranking** | 2nd largest U.S. steel producer | 1st largest U.S. steel producer |
| **Wartime contract ranking** | 7th among all U.S. corporations | Higher overall ranking |
| **Shipbuilding** | 1,121 ships (most of any builder) | Minimal shipbuilding |
| **Specialized military output** | 70% airplane cylinder forgings, 25% armor plate, 33% cannon forgings | Focused on raw steel production |

- While U.S. Steel produced more total tonnage, **Bethlehem dominated specialized military production** -- shipbuilding, armor plate, gun forgings, and ordnance. [^13]
- Bethlehem Steel was the **nation's largest defense contractor** by war's end in 1945, despite being the second-largest steel producer. [^14]
- Bethlehem's advantage was vertical integration: it mined the ore, smelted the steel, forged the components, and built the ships. [^2]

## Post-War Transition

- Unlike many defense contractors, Bethlehem did **not face an immediate demand collapse** after WWII. Global demand for steel surged due to consumer goods (automobiles, household products) and reconstruction of war-torn economies. [^21]
- Bethlehem built **new furnaces and mills** at many plants; by the late 1950s, annual capacity reached **23 million tons**. [^21]
- Eugene Grace stated: **"I have no qualms about excess capacity. The United States will never catch up to its material needs and aspirations."** [^21]
- **1957 peak**: 19+ million tons of steel produced; **$190 million in earnings** on **$2.6 billion in sales** with 165,000+ employees. [^21]
- However, the relentless expansion philosophy created **structural overcapacity** that became a major vulnerability when foreign competition and minimills emerged in the 1960s-1970s. [^21]
- Most wartime shipyards were wound down or converted: Bethlehem-Fairfield closed in 1945 and became a petrochemical site; Bethlehem-Hingham closed after the war. [^15][^17]

# The Schwab Era and Rise to Dominance (1904--1939)

## Summary

Charles M. Schwab transformed Bethlehem Steel from an underperforming company valued under $9 million into the second-largest steel producer in the United States. After resigning as president of U.S. Steel in 1903--1904 due to clashes with J.P. Morgan and Elbert Gary, Schwab took control of Bethlehem Steel and pursued an aggressive strategy of technological innovation, vertical integration, and wartime contracting. His $5 million bet on Henry Grey's wide-flange beam mill---a technology that U.S. Steel had rejected---gave Bethlehem a monopoly on structural steel shapes that enabled the skyscraper age, supplying an estimated 80% of the steel in New York City's skyline during the 1920s. World War I transformed the company from a mid-sized producer with 1.1 million tons of annual capacity and 15,600 employees into an international munitions and shipbuilding colossus, earning $110 million during the war years (six times its earnings in the prior nine years combined) and seeing its stock price rise from $30 to over $600. Wartime profits funded a massive interwar acquisition spree---Lackawanna Steel ($60 million, 1922), Midvale Steel & Ordnance (1923), McClintic-Marshall ($32 million, 1931)---that boosted capacity to 8.5 million tons and employment past 60,000 by the late 1920s. The Great Depression brought the first quarterly loss since 1909 (September 1931) and cumulative losses exceeding $30 million over nine quarters, while executive compensation controversies---particularly Eugene Grace's $1.6 million bonus in 1929---and the Nye Committee's war profiteering investigation damaged the company's public reputation. Schwab died on September 18, 1939, technically bankrupt, with debts exceeding assets by $300,000, his personal fortune consumed by extravagant spending. His protege Eugene Grace continued as president until 1945.

## Key Findings

### 1. Charles M. Schwab's Acquisition and Vision

- Schwab became president of Carnegie Steel at age 35 in 1897 and negotiated the $480 million sale to J.P. Morgan's financiers in 1901, becoming the first president of U.S. Steel Corporation.[^1]
- He resigned from U.S. Steel in 1903--1904 after repeated clashes with Elbert Gary, who headed the executive committee and "had no real knowledge of the steel business" but "forever opposed me on some of the methods," according to Schwab.[^2]
- Personal factors contributed to his departure: extravagant spending on his $7 million "Riverside" mansion in Manhattan, gambling losses at Monte Carlo, an extramarital affair, and deteriorating health.[^2]
- Schwab had strategically purchased Bethlehem Steel as a private investment while still at U.S. Steel, acquiring a controlling interest in 1901 for what he later described as an underperforming company valued under $9 million.[^1][^2]
- His bold declaration upon taking control: he would "make the Bethlehem plant the greatest armor plate and gun factory in the world."[^3]
- He replaced the conservative Packer family leadership with 15 young mill executives, introduced merit-based advancement and performance bonuses, and modernized operations with open-hearth technology.[^2]
- Key cost advantage: Bethlehem's Cuban ore cost $4.31/ton versus U.S. Steel's $7.10/ton, and the ore naturally contained nickel, enabling free nickel steel production.[^2]

### 2. Bethlehem Steel Corporation Formation (1904)

- Bethlehem Steel Corporation was incorporated in New Jersey on December 10, 1904, to effect the reorganization of the troubled United States Shipbuilding Company of 1902.[^4]
- The new corporation was a holding company controlling the older Bethlehem Steel Company and a series of shipyards in Elizabeth, NJ; Wilmington, DE; and San Francisco, CA, plus a Cuban iron ore mine.[^4][^5]
- Schwab served as both first president (1905--1916) and first chairman of the board (1905--1939).[^6]
- In 1905, Bethlehem's ingot capacity represented less than 1% of the national total---a measure of just how far it had to climb.[^5]

### 3. The Wide-Flange Beam Revolution

- In 1897, inventor Henry Grey and fellow steel man George Burrell devised a method using horizontal and vertical rollers to produce a wide-edged beam from a single steel ingot, eliminating the need for costly riveted assemblies.[^7]
- Schwab had tried and failed to convince U.S. Steel to produce the beams. Andrew Carnegie himself dismissed the idea: "Pioneering don't pay."[^7]
- After leaving U.S. Steel, Schwab championed Grey's design at Bethlehem. In December 1905, Bethlehem announced it would build the Grey mill.[^7]
- Investment: approximately $5 million for plant design, construction, and royalties.[^2]
- Schwab's famous remark on the gamble: "I've thought the whole thing over, and if we are going bust, we will go bust big."[^1]
- In January 1908, Bethlehem began producing America's first commercially successful wide-flange structural shapes.[^6]
- The first major project using Bethlehem beams was Gimbel's department store in New York City, consuming 12,000 tons of Grey beams.[^6]
- By August 1909, the Grey mill's products were the company's biggest money makers.[^7]
- Bethlehem Steel was the sole producer of wide-flange beams in the United States until 1927, when U.S. Steel began rolling "Carnegie Beams."[^7]
- By the 1920s, Bethlehem beams were used in an estimated 80% of New York City's skyscrapers, including Rockefeller Center and Madison Square Garden.[^3][^8]

### 4. Shipbuilding Expansion

- The 1904 incorporation already included shipbuilding concerns in California (Union Iron Works in San Francisco) and Delaware.[^4]
- Union Iron Works was formally sold to Bethlehem Steel in 1905--1906 but continued operating under its original name until 1917.[^9]
- In 1913, Bethlehem acquired the Fore River Shipbuilding Company in Quincy, Massachusetts, a major warship producer. The shipbuilding division's main office moved to Quincy.[^9][^10]
- In 1917, the shipbuilding operations were incorporated as the Bethlehem Shipbuilding Corporation, Ltd., consolidating Fore River, Union Iron Works, and other facilities under a single entity.[^9]
- During WWI, Schwab was appointed Director General of the Emergency Fleet Corporation on April 16, 1918, overseeing the national wartime shipbuilding program.[^11]
- Between 1917 and 1918, thirteen shipbuilding contracts were executed (seven on February 1, 1918, and six thereafter), with actual cost to Bethlehem of approximately $109 million.[^11]
- Bethlehem produced more than 65% of the total number of finished artillery pieces for Allied forces between April 1917 and November 1918.[^10]

### 5. Financial Performance During WWI Boom

- At WWI's outset (1914), Bethlehem had annual steelmaking capacity of 1.1 million tons and employed 15,600 people.[^10]
- Pre-war (1910--1914) average annual earnings: approximately $6 million.[^12]
- Wartime (1914--1918) average annual profit: approximately $49 million---an 8-fold increase.[^12]
- Total wartime earnings (1914--1918): approximately $110 million, six times more than the company earned in the nine years preceding the war.[^5][^12]
- Pre-war gross sales: $135 million. By war's end, gross sales had skyrocketed to $1.33 billion.[^12]
- Bethlehem received a $135 million order from Britain---the largest single order in the company's history to that point.[^3]
- The company invested over $102 million in new facility construction during the war.[^10]
- Stock price rose from $30 to over $600 during the war years.[^5]
  - On April 7, 1915, the stock hit 87 3/4 before beginning its parabolic ascent.[^13]
  - By October 4, 1915, it traded at $399.[^13]
  - Legendary speculator Jesse Livermore reportedly turned $500,000 into $15 million trading Bethlehem Steel in nine months during the war boom.[^13]
- By 1918, South Bethlehem was packed with 31,000 workers producing guns, shells, armor plate, submarine parts, and other materiel.[^12]

### 6. War Profiteering Controversies

- The Nye Committee (officially the U.S. Senate Special Committee Investigating the Munitions Industry) was formed in 1934 under Senate Resolution 206, chaired by Republican Senator Gerald P. Nye of North Dakota.[^14]
- The committee held 93 public hearings between September 1934 and February 1936, interrogating over 200 witnesses.[^14]
- The investigation highlighted practices such as cost-plus contracts and lobbying by firms including DuPont and Bethlehem Steel.[^14]
- Combined profits for major munitions firms from 1914 to 1918 reached $1.2 billion (DuPont reported 1,000% profit margins on gunpowder).[^14]
- Eugene G. Grace testified before the committee, defending private industry's role: "Private industry should be aided and encouraged in time of war and in my opinion should not be subject to conscription the same as manpower."[^14]
- The committee found no evidence that munitions manufacturers had more lobbyists or influence than other special interest groups, and failed to prove collusion in naval contract bidding.[^14]
- The committee issued seven reports and recommended legislation to bring the munitions industry under government control---contributing to the passage of the Neutrality Acts of 1935--1937.[^14]
- The U.S. government later sued Bethlehem Steel over wartime shipbuilding contract profits; the case (United States v. Bethlehem Steel Corporation, 315 U.S. 289) reached the Supreme Court in 1942.[^11]

### 7. Interwar Expansion (1916--1931)

- **1916**: Acquired American Iron and Steel Manufacturing Company, Pennsylvania Steel Company, and Maryland Steel Company---all funded by wartime profits. The Pennsylvania Steel acquisition brought the Steelton plant and the massive Sparrows Point, Maryland, works.[^5][^10]
- **1922**: Purchased Lackawanna Steel Company for $60 million, including railroad and coal holdings and Great Lakes iron ore mines. Bethlehem spent over $40 million during the 1920s modernizing the Lackawanna plant, targeting the growing automobile market via Buffalo's proximity to Detroit.[^15]
- **1923**: Acquired Midvale Steel and Ordnance Company and Cambria Steel Company (June 1923).[^5][^15]
- **1925**: Steelmaking capacity reached 8.5 million tons annually; employment exceeded 60,000.[^10]
- **1920s**: Labor force had doubled every five years from 1905 to 1920, reaching 20,000 in the Lehigh Valley alone by 1920.[^2]
- **1931**: Acquired McClintic-Marshall Corporation for $32 million, the largest independent steel fabricator in the country. This enabled Bethlehem to participate in construction of the Golden Gate Bridge, Rockefeller Center, the Waldorf Astoria Hotel, the Chicago Merchandise Mart, and the U.S. Supreme Court Building.[^16]
- **Mid-1930s**: Spent approximately $20 million on a continuous strip and tin-plate mill at Sparrows Point.[^5]
- Also established smaller steelmaking plants in San Francisco, Los Angeles, and Seattle during the 1930s.[^5]

### 8. Great Depression Impact

- **September 1931**: Bethlehem posted a quarterly loss for the first time since 1909.[^5]
- The company had overexpanded and was forced to shut down many facilities, including a newly constructed jumbo-sized open hearth at Sparrows Point.[^5]
- Over nine quarters, cumulative losses exceeded $30 million.[^5]
- **December 1933**: Bethlehem reported a modest net profit in excess of $600,000, the first profit after those nine devastating quarters.[^5]
- Workers showed up at the plant daily but were not guaranteed work or a 40-hour week. Conditions were described as "horrible."[^17]
- The National Industrial Recovery Act of 1933 and the NRA codes provided some relief, establishing minimum wages, maximum work hours, and workers' rights to organize.[^17]
- Despite the downturn, Bethlehem continued selective expansion, creating smaller plants on the West Coast and acquiring McClintic-Marshall.[^5]

### 9. Eugene Grace: Schwab's Protege

- Born August 27, 1876, in Cape May, New Jersey. Graduated from Lehigh University in 1899 as valedictorian.[^18]
- Reportedly declined a contract with the Boston Braves baseball team to start as a crane operator at Bethlehem Steel.[^18]
- Named president of Bethlehem Steel in 1916 at age 39, when Schwab moved to the chairman role. He served as president until 1945 and then chairman until 1957.[^18]
- Tripled the company's steel output during World War I.[^18]
- By 1929, he was the highest-paid executive in America, receiving a bonus in excess of $1.6 million---approximately 3.3% of company earnings.[^5]
- In early 1931, a group of stockholders sued Schwab and 12 other officers, charging that the bonus program constituted misuse of company funds. The suit demanded the return of $36 million in bonuses distributed since 1911.[^5]
- In July 1931, the case was settled, with the requirement that executive bonuses be published in annual reports going forward.[^5]
- In 1941, Grace received the second-highest salary and compensation package in the U.S.: $522,637.[^18]
- Known for "strident anti-unionism" and training generations of executives that "there was only one way for steel to be made: Bethlehem Steel's way."[^18]
- Apocryphal quote attributed to Grace upon hearing of WWII's outbreak: "Gentlemen, we are going to make a lot of money."[^18]
- Generous philanthropist to Lehigh University and the Bethlehem community; his home was designated a historic landmark in 1997.[^18]
- Died July 25, 1960, in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania, at age 83.[^18]

### 10. Charles Schwab's Final Years

- Peak personal fortune estimated at $25--40 million (approximately $500--800 million in early 21st-century dollars).[^1]
- Even before the 1929 crash, lavish spending had depleted his wealth.[^1]
- The 75-room "Riverside" mansion (cost $7 million to build, later valued at $3 million) was seized by creditors.[^1]
- Schwab died September 18, 1939, in New York City from heart disease, at age 77.[^1]
- His estate showed assets of $1.4 million and debts of $1.7 million---technically bankrupt.[^19]
- The Riverside mansion sat vacant, was offered to and rejected by Mayor Fiorello La Guardia as a mayoral residence, and was purchased by Prudential Insurance for $1.5 million in 1947 before being demolished.[^19]
- Near death, Andrew Carnegie had written to Schwab: "I cannot help feeling proud of you for having far outstripped any."[^2]

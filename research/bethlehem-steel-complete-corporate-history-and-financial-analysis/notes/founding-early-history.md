# Founding & Early History of Bethlehem Steel (1857--1904)

## Summary

Bethlehem Steel's origins trace to the Saucona Iron Company, incorporated on April 8, 1857, by Augustus Wolle, a Moravian merchant in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania. The Panic of 1857 immediately stalled the venture. After reorganization under railroad interests led by Robert H. Sayre (chief engineer of the Lehigh Valley Railroad), the company was renamed Bethlehem Iron Company on May 1, 1861, and began producing iron railroad rails during the Civil War. The company transitioned to steel via the Bessemer process in 1873, becoming a significant rail producer (5.2% of national steel rails in 1875, rising to 11.5% by 1879). As rail market share eroded in the 1880s under pressure from Carnegie and Lackawanna Steel, Bethlehem pivoted decisively to military ordnance --- securing the U.S. Navy's first armor plate and forging contracts on June 28, 1887, and completing America's first heavy-forging plant by 1892. This defense pivot sustained the company through the 1893 depression. In 1899, majority stockholders Joseph Wharton and Robert Packer Linderman reorganized the business as Bethlehem Steel Company. Charles M. Schwab acquired controlling interest in 1901, and in December 1904 formed Bethlehem Steel Corporation, which he led as first president and chairman --- setting the stage for its rise to become America's second-largest steelmaker.

## Key Findings

### 1. Saucona Iron Company Origins (1857)

- **Founded**: April 8, 1857, by Augustus Wolle, a prominent Moravian merchant in Bethlehem, PA, who had also co-founded the Thomas Iron Company in 1854.[^1][^2]
- **Name**: "The Saucona Iron Company," named after Saucon Creek where the works were to be built.[^2]
- **Why Bethlehem**: Strategic location at the junction of the Lehigh Valley Railroad and the North Pennsylvania Railroad, near iron ore deposits and anthracite coal supplies.[^1][^3]
- **Initial plan**: Build an anthracite iron works to produce railroad rails.[^3]
- **Immediate failure**: The Panic of 1857 made it impossible to sell sufficient stock; the project "fell into abeyance."[^1]
- **Reorganization**: Revived as "Bethlehem Rolling Mills and Iron Company" with capital infusions from Lehigh Valley Railroad interests. A new board formed June 14, 1860, with Alfred Hunt of Philadelphia as president.[^1]

### 2. Key Founding Figures

**Augustus Wolle** --- Original founder (1857). Moravian merchant and industrialist in Bethlehem. Co-founder of Thomas Iron Company (1854). His vision launched what became Bethlehem Steel, though he did not lead the reorganized company.[^2][^4]

**Asa Packer** --- Railroad magnate, founder and president of the Lehigh Valley Railroad. Served as director on the first board (formed June 1860). Authorized Sayre's mill project. His railroad interests were the financial backbone behind the reorganized company.[^5][^6]

**Robert H. Sayre** (1824--1907) --- Vice president and chief engineer of the Lehigh Valley Railroad. Co-founder and vice president/general manager of Bethlehem Iron Company. Led the 1860 reorganization that rescued the Saucona Iron Company. Responsible for design and construction of the first iron works (1861--1863). Recruited John Fritz. One of the most consequential figures in the company's early decades.[^7][^6]

**John Fritz** (1822--1913) --- "The father of the U.S. steel industry." Arrived in Bethlehem on July 5, 1860, at age 37, recruited by Robert Sayre. Appointed General Superintendent and Chief Engineer. Had previously patented the three-high rail mill (1858) and built Cambria Iron Works into a major producer. Compensation: $5,000/year plus 40 shares of company stock, with 20 additional shares annually. Designed the first U.S. heavy-forging plant (1888--1892). Remained with the company until 1892. Introduced open-hearth steelmaking in the 1880s. Died in Bethlehem in 1913 at age 90.[^5][^8][^9]

**Joseph Wharton** (1826--1909) --- Philadelphia industrialist and philanthropist (founder of the Wharton School at the University of Pennsylvania). Began investing in Bethlehem Iron in the 1870s. Rose to become the largest shareholder and a director. Supported the company's diversification into armor plate. Co-organized the 1899 transition to Bethlehem Steel Company with Robert Packer Linderman. Co-founded Bethlehem Steel Corporation with Schwab in 1904.[^10][^11]

**Robert Packer Linderman** --- President of Bethlehem Iron Company. With Joseph Wharton, orchestrated the 1899 reorganization into Bethlehem Steel Company.[^1]

**Russell Davenport** --- Joined Bethlehem Iron in 1888 to assist John Fritz in designing and building the heavy-forging plant. Later became a key technical leader.[^1]

### 3. Bethlehem Iron Company --- Transition from Iron to Bessemer Steel

- **Renamed**: Bethlehem Iron Company on May 1, 1861 (from Bethlehem Rolling Mills and Iron Company).[^1]
- **First blast furnace**: Construction began July 1, 1861; commenced operations January 4, 1863.[^1]
- **First rolling mill**: Built between spring 1861 and summer 1863.[^1]
- **First railroad rails rolled**: September 26, 1863.[^1]
- **Machine shop completed**: 1865.[^1]
- **Second blast furnace**: Added 1867.[^1]
- **Bessemer process adopted**: 1873 --- Bethlehem Iron began producing steel using the Bessemer converter, becoming one of the first American steel mills to incorporate this process. The nation's tenth Bessemer steel rail mill.[^3][^12]
- **Open-hearth steelmaking**: Introduced by John Fritz in the 1880s. Fritz was never a strong believer in the Bessemer process and favored the more versatile open-hearth method. Open-hearth furnaces began operations in August 1888.[^8][^13]
- **Bessemer converters replaced**: Eventually fully replaced by open-hearth furnaces, which allowed quality testing over a 24-hour period and produced more uniform steel.[^12]

### 4. Early Products

- **Railroad rails** (1863 onward): The primary product for the first two decades. Initially produced exclusively for the Lehigh Valley Railroad, later sold broadly.[^5][^1]
- **Structural steel**: Early production of iron and steel structural members, though the famous wide-flange "Bethlehem Beam" came later (1908).[^14]
- **Heavy forgings**: After 1888, gun forgings, shaft forgings, and marine components.[^1]
- **Armor plate**: Harvey-process nickel-steel armor for U.S. Navy warships (from 1890).[^1][^15]
- **Tool steels**: For metal cutting.[^16]
- **Forgings for electrical generators**: Part of the late-1890s product diversification.[^16]
- **Ferris wheel axle**: In 1893, manufactured the 45.5-foot, 89,320-pound (71-ton) hollow steel axle for the world's first Ferris wheel at the Chicago World's Columbian Exposition --- the largest single steel forging ever constructed at the time. Arrived in Chicago on March 18, 1893.[^1][^17]

### 5. Government Contracts --- Naval Armor Plate

- **Strategic context**: The U.S. Navy's 1881 decision to rebuild with steam-driven, steel-hulled warships fundamentally reshaped Bethlehem Iron's destiny.[^1]
- **1885 mission**: John Fritz, Robert Sayre, William Thurston, and Joseph Wharton met with Lt. William Jaques in Philadelphia. Jaques had negotiated with Joseph Whitworth's Manchester firm regarding heavy-forging technology.[^1]
- **1886 Congressional authorization**: Spring 1886, Congress passed a naval appropriations bill authorizing construction of two armored second-class battleships (USS Texas and USS Maine with 12-inch and 10-inch guns), one protected cruiser, one first-class torpedo boat, and modernization of two Civil War-era monitors.[^1]
- **Contract awarded**: June 28, 1887 --- Bethlehem secured both the forging and armor contracts.[^1]
- **Heavy-forging plant**: Designed by John Fritz with Russell Davenport's assistance. Completed between 1888 and 1892. America's first heavy-forging plant.[^1]
- **Gun forgings delivered**: By fall 1890, Bethlehem was delivering gun forgings and completing armor-plating facilities.[^1]
- **Armor plate scandal (1893)**: Both Bethlehem Iron and Carnegie Steel received contracts in February/March 1893 for approximately 6,500 tons of nickel-steel armor treated via the Harvey process, at $50.40--$100.80 per ton additional cost depending on plate thickness. Charles Schwab (then at Carnegie) was found to have patched blowholes in armor plate when government inspectors were absent. President Cleveland levied a fine of $140,484.94 (reduced from 15% to 10% of contract value).[^15]
- **Significance**: The armor contracts transformed Bethlehem from a declining regional rail producer into the nation's preeminent defense contractor, sustaining it through the 1893--1898 depression.[^1][^3]

### 6. The Grey Rolling Mill

- **Henry Grey's patent**: 1897, for a process to roll wide-flange structural steel beams.[^14]
- **Bethlehem's adoption**: Announced December 1905; became the first manufacturer to adopt Grey's innovative mill rolling process.[^14]
- **First wide-flange beams rolled**: January 9, 1908.[^14]
- **Product**: Wide-flange "H" beams (later patented as "Bethlehem Beams") --- lighter, cheaper, and more structurally sound than traditional built-up "I" beams. Described as "stronger, lighter, and less expensive" than fabricated alternatives.[^14][^16]
- **Impact**: Enabled modern skyscraper construction. While this falls just outside the 1857--1904 scope, Grey's patent (1897) and the decision to invest in this technology were made during the period under study.

### 8. Competitive Position

- **Rail market**: In the 1870s, Bethlehem was a significant but not dominant rail producer (5.2% in 1875, 11.5% in 1879). By the early 1880s, its rail market share was declining "in the face of competition from growing Pittsburgh and Scranton-based firms, such as the Carnegie Steel Company and Lackawanna Steel."[^3]
- **Strategic differentiation**: While Carnegie dominated bulk steel (rails, structural shapes, plate) through massive scale and vertical integration, Bethlehem pivoted to high-value defense and specialty products --- armor plate, heavy forgings, ordnance --- where it had no domestic competitor until Carnegie entered the armor market in the early 1890s.[^1][^15]
- **By 1904**: Bethlehem Steel Corporation had ingot capacity of less than 1% of national production, far behind U.S. Steel (formed 1901 from Carnegie Steel and others). However, it held a dominant position in naval ordnance and heavy forgings.[^16]
- **Key distinction**: Carnegie/U.S. Steel competed on volume and cost in commodity steel. Bethlehem competed on technical capability in specialty and defense products --- a niche that would fuel its rapid growth under Schwab and Grace after 1904.

### 9. Frederick Winslow Taylor at Bethlehem (1898--1901)

- Taylor joined Bethlehem Steel in May 1898 as a management consultant at $35/day plus living expenses, hired to solve a machine-shop capacity problem.[^18]
- Conducted his famous pig iron handling experiments: increased average output from 12.5 tons/worker/day to 47.5 tons through time-and-motion study, worker selection, and restructured incentives. Worker pay increased 60%.[^18]
- The experiments became foundational case studies in the development of scientific management, though Taylor revised his accounts multiple times, and the methods were controversial even at the time.[^18]

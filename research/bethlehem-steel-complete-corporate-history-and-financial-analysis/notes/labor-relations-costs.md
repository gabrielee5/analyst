# Labor Relations, Union History & Cost Structure Evolution

## Summary

Bethlehem Steel's labor history is a century-long arc from brutal exploitation to union power to crippling legacy costs. The company's workforce endured 12-hour days and seven-day weeks in the early 1900s, fought for recognition through violent strikes in 1910 and 1919, and finally organized under the Steel Workers Organizing Committee (SWOC) in 1941. From the late 1940s through 1959, a cycle of industry-wide strikes won steelworkers ever-richer contracts -- wages, pensions, healthcare, supplemental unemployment benefits, and 13-week sabbaticals. The 1973 Experimental Negotiating Agreement (ENA) traded the right to strike for guaranteed 3% annual raises plus COLA, locking in cost escalation just as foreign competition intensified. By 1982, total steelworker compensation reached $26.29/hour including fringes -- 60% above the average factory worker. The cost structure became terminal: at bankruptcy in 2001, Bethlehem had only 13,000 active employees supporting 95,000 retirees, spending $750 million annually on pensions and retiree healthcare. The PBGC assumed $3.7 billion in unfunded pension liabilities -- the largest takeover in the agency's history.

## Key Findings

### 1. Early Labor Conditions (Pre-Union Era)

- **12-hour days were standard.** Over 97% of the Bethlehem workforce had a workday of 10+ hours; 51% worked 12 hours or more. Blast furnace and boiler workers averaged 84 hours per week. Twenty-nine percent worked seven-day weeks with no extra pay for Sundays.[^1]
- **Base pay was subsistence-level.** Laborers earned 12 cents per hour (~$3.75/hr in 2024 dollars). The "bonus system" for skilled workers paid so little in base wages that overtime was effectively compulsory.[^1]
- **Working conditions were deadly.** Steel mills were characterized as "hot, loud, and dangerous." Workers faced extreme heat from blast furnaces, molten metal splashes, heavy machinery hazards, and toxic fumes with virtually no safety protections.[^2]

### 2. The 1910 Strike

- **Duration:** 108 days, beginning February 4, 1910.[^1]
- **Scope:** Over 9,000 workers walked out at the Bethlehem, PA plant.[^1]
- **Demands:** Sundays off, raise from 12 cents/hour, reform of the bonus system.[^1]
- **Outcome:** The strike itself failed -- workers returned without concessions. But the resulting federal investigation led to HR 9061, signed by President Taft in June 1912, the first federal law regulating working hours in American industry. Any company with a federal contract was required to provide 8-hour days.[^3]
- **Significance:** Though a tactical defeat, it was a strategic victory -- it put Bethlehem's labor practices under national scrutiny and established the precedent for federal labor regulation.

### 3. The 1919 Great Steel Strike

- **Duration:** September 22, 1919 to January 8, 1920 (~108 days).[^4]
- **Scope:** 350,000 steelworkers nationwide, spanning Chicago, St. Louis, Pittsburgh, and Bethlehem.[^4]
- **Issues:** Union recognition, the eight-hour day, improved wages.[^4]
- **Violence at Bethlehem:** Strikers threw stones at company police, who opened fire with revolvers, killing steelworker Casimir Mazurek; Maciez Buczkowski died from wounds two days later.[^5]
- **Outcome:** Complete defeat. The strike was broken by scabs brought in under military protection. Led by Elbert H. Gary (U.S. Steel president), the industry held firm. Almost no union organizing occurred in steel for the next 15 years.[^4]
- **Context:** Bethlehem had been the largest single supplier of weapons and munitions to the Allies in WWI, generating record profits under president Eugene Grace, which made the contrast between executive wealth and worker conditions even more politically charged.[^5]

### 4. Unionization (1937-1942)

- **1937 Little Steel Strike:** Bethlehem was one of the four "Little Steel" companies (along with Republic Steel, Inland Steel, and Youngstown Sheet & Tube) that refused to follow U.S. Steel's recognition of SWOC. The Memorial Day Massacre at Republic Steel's Chicago plant (May 30, 1937) killed 10 strikers and injured 30.[^6]
- **February 1941:** Bethlehem's Lackawanna, NY plant workers won a collective bargaining vote after a 39-hour strike.[^7]
- **March 1941:** SWOC organized a four-day walkout at the Bethlehem, PA plant (March 24-28, 1941).[^7]
- **August 1941:** Under federal government pressure during wartime mobilization, Bethlehem Steel agreed that SWOC was selected to represent its workers.[^7]
- **May 22, 1942:** SWOC and the Amalgamated Association (AA) disbanded and reformed as the United Steelworkers of America (USWA) at a convention in Cleveland.[^8]

### 5. The Postwar Strike Cycle (1946-1959)

Each strike won richer contracts, ratcheting up the cost structure:

**1946 Strike**
- **Duration:** ~27 days (January 21 to mid-February 1946)[^9]
- **Scope:** 750,000 steelworkers nationwide (USWA-wide)[^9]
- **Outcome:** 18.5 cents/hour wage increase. President Truman intervened. Wage board also approved matching increases for all white-collar steel employees earning under $5,000/year.[^9]

**1949 Strike**
- Industry-wide stoppage focused on pensions and insurance. Won employer-funded pension plans.

**1952 Strike**
- **Duration:** 53 days (after Supreme Court overturned Truman's seizure)[^10]
- **Constitutional crisis:** On April 8, 1952, Truman issued Executive Order 10340 seizing the entire steel industry. The Supreme Court ruled the seizure unconstitutional in *Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer* -- a landmark separation-of-powers case.[^10]
- **Outcome:** 16 cents/hour wage increase plus improved pensions and insurance -- total cost to industry estimated at 21 cents/hour. Settlement essentially matched the union's November 1951 proposal.[^10]

**1955-1956 Strikes**
- David McDonald led the Steelworkers on strike in 1956, winning substantial wage increases, Supplemental Unemployment Benefits (SUB), COLA (cost-of-living adjustments), layoff rights, and improved pensions.[^11]
- The USWA estimated the COLA provision alone was worth $250 million over the life of the contract.[^11]

**1959 Strike -- The Turning Point**
- **Duration:** 116 days (July 15 to November 7, 1959) -- the longest in steel history.[^12]
- **Scope:** 500,000 USWA members nationwide.[^12]
- **Core issue:** Management demanded the union surrender contract clauses limiting management's ability to change work rules, reassign workers, or introduce labor-saving machinery.[^12]
- **Government intervention:** Eisenhower invoked Taft-Hartley Act; federal judge issued 80-day injunction; USWA appealed to Supreme Court, which upheld the injunction.[^12]
- **Outcome:** Workers won a wage increase and retained work-rule protections. But the real consequences were catastrophic for the industry:
  - **Imports surged:** Steel imports jumped from 2 million tons (1958) to 5 million tons (1959). This was the first year U.S. steel imports exceeded exports.[^13]
  - **Customer flight:** Steel users, burned by the 116-day shutdown, began permanent diversification to foreign suppliers and substitute materials.
  - **Minimill opening:** The strike strengthened the competitive position of nonunion domestic producers.[^12]

### 6. Pattern Bargaining and the Wage Escalation Trap

The USWA pursued industry-wide "pattern bargaining" -- negotiating a single contract with one major producer (usually U.S. Steel), then applying identical terms across all companies. By 1954, the system had eliminated the southern wage differential, creating "virtually a single wage scale for almost all steelmaking operations under contract with the USWA, irrespective of size, relative profitability, or geographical location."[^13]

**Key dynamics:**
- Wages were taken "out of competition" -- every steelworker doing the same job got the same pay regardless of employer.
- Profitable companies like Nucor subsidized the structure; marginal producers like Bethlehem's older plants were crushed by uniform costs.
- Each contract ratcheted upward: wage increases, COLA, SUB, pensions, healthcare, 13-week sabbaticals for senior workers.[^13]

### 7. The 1973 Experimental Negotiating Agreement (ENA)

- **Terms:** USWA surrendered the right to strike in exchange for guaranteed 3% annual wage increases plus cost-of-living adjustments (COLA). Disputes went to binding arbitration.[^14]
- **Rationale:** The postwar strike cycle (1946, 1949, 1952, 1956, 1959) had driven customers to foreign suppliers. Both sides hoped stability would stem import growth.
- **Fatal flaw:** COLA increases were tied to national inflation, not steel industry productivity. When inflation surged in the 1970s, compensation costs spiraled upward regardless of company profitability.[^14]
- **Assessment:** "The pact was hailed in many places as a breakthrough in labor relations but it only accelerated the distress." It locked in cost escalation at precisely the moment the industry needed flexibility to compete with Japanese imports and domestic minimills.[^13]

- Eugene Grace "failed to adequately invest in the company's pension plans during the 1950s," creating a structural deficit that compounded over decades.[^15]
- The net present value of Bethlehem's legacy benefits (excluding pensions) was approximately $3 billion at bankruptcy.[^18]
- When the PBGC took over, the "30-years-and-out" early retirement provision was eliminated. Retirees under 62 lost benefits entirely until reaching standard retirement age.[^19]

### 10. Retiree Healthcare (OPEB) -- The Legacy Cost Crisis

- At bankruptcy, Bethlehem provided healthcare coverage for 130,000 people, including approximately 95,000 retiree beneficiaries.[^18]
- OPEB obligations grew from $1.82 billion (1999) to $2.05 billion (2001).[^17]
- A U.S. Bankruptcy Court judge ruled in early 2003 that Bethlehem could terminate all health and life insurance benefits for its 95,000 retirees, effective March 31, 2003.[^20]
- ISG (International Steel Group) acquired Bethlehem's assets for $1.5 billion specifically structured to "purchase assets and shed liabilities such as workers' health care, insurance and pensions."[^18]

### 11. The Ratio Problem: Retirees vs. Active Workers

| Year | Active Employees | Retirees/Dependents | Ratio |
|------|-----------------|---------------------|-------|
| ~1945 | 300,000 | Minimal | N/A |
| Late 1950s | 165,000 | Growing | ~1:1 |
| 1975 | ~115,000 | Growing | -- |
| 1983 | <60,000 | >60,000 | **Crossover: more pensioners than workers** |
| Mid-1980s | ~48,500 | Growing | ~2:1 retirees per worker |
| Late 1990s | ~15,600 | ~95,000 | ~6:1 |
| 2001 (bankruptcy) | 13,000 | 95,000 | **~7:1** (some sources say 10:1 including dependents) |

- "For each active employee, Bethlehem provided health care coverage for more than seven retirees."[^18]
- Including dependents, the ratio was approximately 10 retirees or dependents per active worker.[^18]
- National comparison: the U.S. had roughly 3 wage-earners per Medicare beneficiary, meaning Bethlehem's burden was approximately 20 times worse than the national average.[^18]

**Key structural differences:**
- "A Nucor minimill with half the tonnage of Sparrows Point operated with one-tenth the workers."[^21]
- Nucor earned $45,400 in profit per employee vs. $9,200 for Bethlehem (1996).[^21]
- Nucor's non-union incentive pay model meant workers who produced more steel earned more -- no flat-rate union wage scale.[^22]
- Bethlehem's legacy costs (pensions + OPEB) added an estimated $50-60+ per ton to production costs, a burden Nucor and foreign competitors did not bear.
- The integrated steelmaking process (coke ovens, blast furnaces, BOF) required massive capital investment and labor vs. Nucor's electric arc furnace (EAF) recycling scrap steel.

### 13. Eugene Grace and Executive Compensation Controversies

- **1929 bonus revelation:** Grace received a base salary of $12,000 and a bonus exceeding $1.6 million -- approximately 3.3% of company earnings. In 2024 dollars, this equates to roughly a $215,000 salary with a $29 million bonus.[^23]
- **Stockholder lawsuit (1931):** A group of stockholders sued Schwab and 12 other officers, charging that the bonus program was a "misuse of company funds" and demanding $36 million in bonuses distributed since 1911 be returned to the company.[^23]
- **Settlement:** No funds were returned, but the suit led to mandatory publication of executive bonuses in annual reports and a revised compensation policy.[^23]
- **Political impact:** Labor unions subsequently used the Grace bonus scandal in their demands for higher rank-and-file compensation, making it a permanent leverage point in negotiations.[^23]
- **Arthur B. Homer (1958):** Earned $511,249 as Bethlehem CEO, making him the highest-paid corporate officer in America that year.[^13]
- **Pension neglect:** Grace's failure to adequately fund pension plans during the profitable 1950s created a structural deficit that haunted the company for decades.[^15]

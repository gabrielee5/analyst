# Research Plan: Bethlehem Steel Corporation

## Core Research Question
How did Bethlehem Steel Corporation — America's second-largest steelmaker, builder of iconic infrastructure, and arsenal of two world wars — rise to industrial dominance and then collapse into liquidation? What financial, strategic, competitive, and structural forces shaped each inflection point across its 146-year history (1857–2003)?

## Sub-Questions
1. What were the founding conditions and early competitive advantages that positioned Bethlehem Steel for growth?
2. How did Charles M. Schwab transform the company after acquiring it in 1904?
3. What was the financial impact of WWI and WWII military contracts on revenue, margins, and capacity?
4. How did labor relations, unionization, and wage/benefit structures evolve, and what was their cost impact?
5. When did import competition and mini-mills begin eroding market share, and how did management respond?
6. What were the key financial metrics (revenue, margins, debt, pension obligations) across each era?
7. How did the stock price perform relative to the S&P 500 and steel sector peers across decades?
8. What strategic decisions (or failures to decide) most contributed to the terminal decline?
9. How did the bankruptcy and liquidation process unfold (2001–2003)?
10. What is Bethlehem Steel's legacy in industrial economics and corporate strategy?

## Subtopics for Deep Dives

### 1. Founding and Early History (1857–1904)
- Saucona Iron Company origins, transition to Bessemer steel
- Key figures: Asa Packer, John Fritz, Robert Sayre
- Early products: railroad rails, structural steel
- Bethlehem Iron Company formation and government contracts
- Sources: Historical society records, business history papers, early corporate records

### 2. The Charles Schwab Era and Rise to Dominance (1904–1939)
- Schwab's acquisition and vision (from US Steel to Bethlehem)
- Grey beam mill and the wide-flange revolution
- Shipbuilding expansion (Fore River, Union Iron Works)
- Financial performance through WWI boom and interwar period
- Sources: Business biographies, Fortune/WSJ archives, Schwab papers

### 3. World Wars: Military Production and Financial Impact
- WWI: ordnance, armor plate, munitions, warship construction
- Profitability during WWI — war profiteering controversies
- WWII: 1,121 ships built, peak employment, government contracts
- Revenue/profit data during wartime vs. peacetime
- Sources: Military history, congressional records, wartime production data

### 4. Post-War Golden Age (1945–1970)
- Infrastructure boom: highways, bridges, skyscrapers
- Iconic projects: Golden Gate Bridge, Rockefeller Center, Waldorf Astoria, Madison Square Garden
- Peak production volumes and employment figures
- Market share and competitive positioning vs. US Steel
- Capital investment decisions (or lack thereof)
- Sources: Company annual reports, industry publications, engineering records

### 5. Labor Relations, Unions, and Cost Structure
- USWA (United Steelworkers) relationship across decades
- Major strikes: 1910, 1919, 1946, 1952, 1959
- Wage and benefit escalation: pattern bargaining
- Pension obligations and retiree healthcare (OPEB) — the ticking time bomb
- Quantify: cost-per-ton vs. mini-mills and imports
- Sources: Labor history archives, BLS data, NLRB records, union contracts

### 6. Competitive Landscape and Import Threat (1960–2000)
- Japanese steel industry rise (Nippon Steel, Kawasaki)
- Korean entry (POSCO — world's most efficient producer by 1980s)
- Mini-mill revolution: Nucor's disruption model
- US government trade protection: VRAs, tariffs, Section 201
- Market share data: Bethlehem vs. peers over time
- Sources: ITC reports, trade commission data, industry analyses

### 7. Financial Performance and Stock Price History
- Revenue, net income, margins across major eras (whatever data is available)
- Stock price history: key peaks and troughs with catalysts
- Capital structure evolution: debt levels, credit ratings
- Dividend history and shareholder returns
- Comparison with S&P 500 and steel sector indices
- Sources: Historical stock databases, annual reports, Moody's/S&P records

### 8. Terminal Decline and Bankruptcy (1990–2003)
- 1990s restructuring attempts under successive CEOs
- Burns Harbor modernization vs. Bethlehem PA plant closure
- Rising pension/OPEB obligations as active workforce shrank
- Chapter 11 filing (October 15, 2001) and proceedings
- ISG acquisition of assets (2003) — what was salvaged
- PBGC pension takeover — largest in history at the time
- Sources: SEC filings, bankruptcy court records, news coverage

## Methodology
- Historical financial analysis with era-by-era segmentation
- Competitive benchmarking against peer group across time periods
- Labor cost analysis and pension obligation modeling
- Stock price analysis with event-driven catalysts
- Narrative corporate history grounded in quantitative data
